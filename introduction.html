Representation in turbulent times

1 Introduction

In recent decades, scholars have found striking evidence for growing political disillusionment and the erosion of democratic norms, leading to a democratic deficit in established democracies (Norris 1997, 2011; Follesdal and Hix 2006). These deficits concern fundamental principles of democratic governance, such as the erosion of trust in the political system (Kriesi 2013) and policies that do not respond to citizens’ preferences (Binzer Hobolt and Klemmensen 2007; Schäfer 2021). Two frequently cited reasons are the marginalization of certain social groups which are consistently underrepresented in political institutions (Mansbridge 1999; Lax and Phillips 2012) and the political disengagement of large parts of society (Jennings et al. 2017; Merkel 2014). Numerous studies have dealt with unequal representation notably of women (Inglehart and Norris 2003; Grey 2006; Schwindt-Bayer 2006; Childs and Webb 2012), race (Broockman 2013; Butler and Broockman 2011) and (income) classes (Carnes 2012; Bartels 2016; Gilens 2012) and also examined intersectional effects (Smooth 2006; Weldon 2006). On the other hand, the representation of different age groups has been more or less neglected. The limited number of contributions found that young adults were consistently under-represented in parliaments, and explained this either by a lack of political participation (Lawless and Fox 2015; Shames 2017) or by restrictive political institutions (Joshi 2013; Stockemer and Sundström 2018). To my knowledge, there are only three direct indications of how young adults’ interests are represented in policy outcomes. Alesina, Cassidy, and Troiano (2018) shows that without term limits, younger mayors are more likely to engage with political budget cycles, suggesting that they have longer time horizons. This is demonstrated in an experimental setting by McClean (2021), who found evidence that Japanese mayors under the age of 50 increased spending on child welfare. Finally, the only study to examine the representation of group attitudes in the political process comes from Kissau, Lutz, and Rosset (2012), who found that the preferences of different age groups are relatively equally represented in the lower house of the Swiss parliament. However, as various institutional factors are thought to influence government responsiveness to citizens’ political demands (Binzer Hobolt and Klemmensen 2007; Soroka and Wlezien 2009; Wratil and Hobolt 2019; Bevan and Jennings 2014), it is worth asking whether these findings are consistent in other contexts. These issues are particularly important given the dramatic demographic changes facing all developed democracies.

Figure 1.1: Share of population per group in OECD countries

Population data from the Organisation for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) from 1996 to 2016 (OECD 2023) presented in figure 1.1 show an unmistakable trend towards an aging society in advanced democracies. Broken down into children, young people, and the elderly, the data show a gradual but consistent decline in the proportion of younger cohorts (15-34 years) and an increase in the proportion of older cohorts (35 years and over), probably due to a combination of factors such as declining fertility rates, increased life expectancy, and declining birth rates. Looking at the details, in 1996 the average proportion of young women was 14.9 percent and that of young men 15 percent. This dynamic experienced a downward trend, with the young female and male populations falling to 14.1 percent and 15.1 percent respectively in 2006, and further to 12.9 percent and 13.8 percent in 2016. At the same time, older women and men accounted for 24.4 percent and 22.5 percent of the population in 1996, rising to 26.6 percent and 24.8 percent in 2006 and further increasing to 28.5 percent and 26.7 percent in 2016. In addition, the decline in the share of children, although less pronounced than in the 15-34 age group, confirms the decline in fertility rates. These shifts in population demographics are not without profound implications, especially in the domain of public expenditure.

A corollary of this demographic transition is a declining share of people in the 15-34 age group, leading to a shortage of new entrants to the labor force. This demographic situation consequently raises the dependency ratio, i.e. the ratio of people of working age to people of non-working age (Lutz, Sanderson, and Scherbov 2008). In response, policies may need to promote the participation of young people in the labor market by ensuring that job opportunities match young people’s qualifications, career preferences, and skills. This requires understanding young people’s values and priorities and treating them as equals. For example, problems may arise from the increasing healthcare needs of an aging population, leading to a corresponding increase in healthcare expenditure. This includes spending on long-term care facilities, health services, and programs for age-related diseases. In addition, the growing number of retired people can put considerable pressure on public finances, particularly in contexts where pension systems are predominantly publicly funded. Coupled with a shrinking tax base for social spending, this calls for strict prioritization of resources to meet the needs of different demographic cohorts. Given their vital role in underpinning the future of society, young people may become more aware of their societal significance, particularly in terms of their critical role in sustaining the economy and the political system. These trends underscore the urgent need for OECD countries to adapt their policy architecture and social spending to a progressively aging population structure. As young people become more important in this aging demographic context, their demands for representation are likely to increase. Conversely, a large body of research has shown that young adults have low political interest and knowledge, a lack of conventional political participation, and a rare presence in political assemblies. The representation of adults aged 35 and under in parliaments around the world was a modest 10 percent in 2017 (Stockemer and Sundstrom 2022). Within the OECD population, their representation in the lower chambers of parliaments (under 40: 21 percent in 2018) is relatively higher compared to the global average. However, young legislators’ presence is far from uniform across OECD countries. For example, although South Korea has a younger population than Denmark, the proportion of young legislators in Denmark (41 percent) is more than twenty times higher than in South Korea (2 percent). Similarly, Italy (33 percent) has almost five times as many younger members of the parliament (MPs) as Japan (7 percent), even though both are among the oldest advanced democracies (McClean 2020a). Stockemer and Sundström (2018) have argued that this lack of adequate representation creates a vicious circle that reinforces the political alienation of young voters.

Before considering the implications of these differences for the political process, we need to ask: Do young people need representation? This is a more complex question than it might appear on the surface. While certain characteristics, such as being short or tall, might be a disadvantage in some contexts, this is not necessarily the case in politics. Furthermore, it could be argued that low representation of young voters is not a systematical disadvantage because their exclusion from representation does not lead to lifelong inequality. This is a condition that is prominent among women (Philips, Anne 1994) and ethnic minorities (Kymlicka 1996). The life-cycle argument claims that age discrimination can be seen as fair in the light of individuals’ access to resources throughout their lives (Parfit 1984; McKerlie 1989; Bidadanure 2015). However, this perspective of distributive equality overlooks the profound relationship between political processes and path dependencies. Political decisions often have an indelible impact on subsequent stages of the life cycle. In this context, the lack of youth representation in politics can be seen as a substantive issue for birth cohorts (Daniels 1983). The unequal treatment leads to inequalities between individuals over time. For example, if one birth cohort receives free higher education while the next cohort has to pay, then the institution has treated these groups unequally throughout their lives. An alternative proposition to the lifecycle argument that is often put forward is that gender equality should not focus solely on a lifecycle perspective, but should extend to different stages of life (McKerlie 1992). This proposition injects a sophisticated interpretation into the debate on equality, countering the conventional emphasis on a lifetime perspective and recognizing that disparate life stages could encounter distinct challenges and opportunities requiring individual attention. However, the theoretical solidity of this approach has been contested. Bidadanure (2016) posits that “complete lives are the least arbitrary segments to apply the value of distributive equality [...and] thus stands on comparatively frail grounds”. With this assertion, Bidadanure brings the argument of relational equality into the field, emphasizing the need for representational structures for disadvantaged groups to eliminate socially enforced oppression (Anderson 1999) and to avert potential unequal distributions (Young 1989). Consequently, relational equality cannot be actualized merely through the distribution of resources throughout a lifetime but requires equal standing and status in social relations for people of different ages.

While the majority of researchers in the field have attempted to isolate these effects, with generational effects considered prima facie problematic and age effects more acceptable (Bidadanure 2016), I argue that understanding the demand for representation in the context of relational equality is essential to the study of youth political representation. This is not only due to the given increases in dependency on this age group, but also a matter of democratic principles. Young individuals are in a distinct life stage with unique interests that require particular consideration. If political decisions are not reflective of those, they do not see and learn the benefits of the democratic process. This could instigate lasting alienation of this group from political participation (Pruitt 2017), which may induce a self-reinforcing cycle (Stockemer and Sundström 2018). Some see the antidote to this cycle in tackling its most visible manifestation: the lack of youth representation in political assemblies (Briggs 2016). I argue that to focus solely on descriptive representation, while taking the field for relational equality, is to misunderstand the goal and the means of achieving that goal. Thus, while numerical representation is an important factor, an overemphasis on this type of representation could overshadow other democratic principles or lead to an undue focus on the characteristics of legislators over policy decisions (Mansbridge 1999). Descriptive representation becomes particularly important when it influences both the decision-making process of the assembly and its legitimacy among the under-represented group, leading to a mismatch between the demand for and supply of representation (Norris and Franklin 1997). The need for descriptive representation should therefore be derived from its potential to enhance substantive representation. So my aim in this thesis is to ask: Is there a gap in the representation of young adults in political outcomes? Furthermore, I ask whether any gaps are mainly due to a lack of political participation or a low number of representatives in assemblies. As age is an issue common to all social groups and societies, and both could explain a lack of representation for this group, this case provides a unique opportunity to disentangle both effects. Before delving further into this analysis, I will continue with the theoretical foundations of my research.
