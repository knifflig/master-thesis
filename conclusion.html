Representation in turbulent times

6 Conclusion

Substantive representation is an integral part of the health and functioning of a democratic system. Numerous academic studies have linked a lack of representation to the emergence of democratic deficits, confirming its importance in society. The relevance of this issue becomes particularly clear when one considers the younger demographic group. Although often overlooked, this segment of the population plays a key role in shaping the socio-political milieu, and its impact is amplified by upcoming demographic shifts. The demographic transitions predict a sharp rise in the dependency ratio, with an emphasis on young people. This reality positions them as the backbone of economic well-being and the center of social transfers. This is coupled with a significant evolution in the belief system of the younger generation, with a pronounced shift toward immaterial values and relational equality. This emerging trend reinforces this group’s demand for substantive representation, a demand that is likely to continue to grow. Understanding the responsiveness of policymakers to the needs of different social groups and identifying the dynamics that can strengthen representation becomes a critical concern in this context. Unlike other societal issues such as income inequality or minorities, age, along with gender, provides a more comprehensive lens for comparison across countries and time. Age transcends societal structures and provides a common feature that permeates societies. With most OECD countries facing aging populations, age-based analysis allows for a greater degree of generalisability. In particular, analyzing the dynamics of income groups depends on the selection and definition of these groups, leading to considerable variation in the results. Conversely, age groups can be clearly delineated by certain cut-off points (30/35/40) and our research confirms the robustness of the results across these groups.2 Despite its relevance and potential, research focusing on age groups remains unduly scarce. My study contributes to this research gap by highlighting two salient aspects. First, I argue that the demand for substantive representation among young voters is far from being met. This conclusion is based on our finding that shifts in government spending are significantly less in line with the preferences of younger citizens than their older counterparts. Furthermore, this research introduces a comprehensive approach that incorporates dynamic and group representation mechanisms. My findings highlight that current assemblies do not adequately address issues relevant to young adults, regardless of their electoral incentives. Thus, the interplay between group representation and dynamic representation theories provides fertile ground for insights into the determinants of political responsiveness in different policy contexts.

Both theories are consistent in explaining the decline in government spending despite an increase in young adults’ preferences for government spending, but they highlight different aspects of the gap between young adults’ preferences and policy outcomes. Politicians, fundamentally driven by electoral behavior (Strøm 1990; Anderson 1999), often have no choice but need to disregard groups that refrain from voting, such as young adults. At the same time, a political system with a predominant focus on short-term policy outcomes fails to adequately address the long-term perspectives and interests of young adults. This mismatch can potentially lead to a sense of political alienation among young adults, thereby creating a self-reinforcing cycle in which politicians cater primarily to groups that exhibit robust political participation. Furthermore, young MPs change their stance over the course of their political careers in order to adapt to the prevailing political process. This tendency can lead to a mismatch between young adults’ preferences and actual policy outcomes. Furthermore, it is argued that young politicians cannot adequately represent their contemporaries due to a lack of skills or systemic barriers within parliaments (Bailer et al. 2021). It’s important to note, however, that empirical evidence suggests that young adults behave differently from their older counterparts and are more likely to represent the interests of their age group (Alesina, Cassidy, and Troiano 2018; McClean 2021). Despite this tendency, the number of young MPs remains disproportionately low (Stockemer and Sundström 2018; Stockemer and Sundstrom 2022).

Even when the preferences of young adults are known to older parliamentarians, a lack of understanding or empathy for these preferences contributes to a lack of representation in policy outcomes. This problem is exacerbated by the shift in young adults’ values towards relational equality and their increased demand for representation, elements that are not reflected in the belief systems of the majority of parliamentarians. As a result, the interests of young adults are often not adequately considered by critical actors (Childs and Webb 2012), underlining the urgency of strengthening descriptive representation. In order to determine whether a shift to long-term perspectives or an increase in descriptive representation would be more beneficial, I examined differences across issue domains. My results suggest that the domain of political issues can significantly influence the degree of political responsiveness. Both theories posited reduced responsiveness in areas of long-term importance to young adults, a prediction that our data confirmed. Politicians show strong responsiveness to issues of importance to the median voter, regardless of the time horizon of these issues. This could be attributed to a greater alignment of interests between the median voter and the typical policymaker in terms of age-related relevance and preferences. Such a trend implies an increased importance of group representation. In addition, policymakers generally show a bias towards short-term issues with immediate benefits. However, our results suggest that this bias may be a less important driver than initially thought. For example, while it is one element of the overall landscape, it does not overshadow the importance of other influencing factors, in particular the need for greater group representation among young adults.

The analysis was further deepened by examining the responsiveness of politicians to different age groups, with a particular focus on young adults. The responsiveness of politicians to the electorate often depends on political participation. In particular, low voter turnout among young people is a problem in this regard (Lawless and Fox 2015; Shames 2017). This trend has led to claims that legislators lack incentives to address the interests of the younger demographic. Young politicians are also often seen as potentially inadequate representatives of their contemporaries due to skill deficits or systemic obstacles within parliamentary structures. Moreover, those young politicians who do manage to overcome these barriers often find themselves having to realign their strategies with the older median voter in order to secure re-election. This paradigm suggests that an actor seeking re-election has little choice but to neglect groups that refrain from voting. This dynamic contributes to the low substantive representation of young adults and other demographic groups characterized by low turnout and places some responsibility for this state of affairs on these groups themselves. However, the findings challenge this paradigm. We can see a pronounced negative responsiveness to issue areas of significant relevance to young adults. On the other hand, short-term effects seem to have a negligible impact on politicians’ responsiveness to young voters. This finding contradicts the hypothesis that politicians are primarily responsive to the interests of groups with high voter turnout, and provides evidence for the importance of group representation factors.

Thus, my findings suggest that vote-seeking strategies are not the primary determinant of politicians’ considerations. Instead, shared characteristics emerge as a more powerful driver. This conclusion underscores the importance of group representation in shaping policy outcomes and challenges the narrative that low turnout among young adults is primarily to blame for underrepresentation. While this contributes to our understanding of the representation of young adults and the underlying drivers of substantive representation, it is important to acknowledge certain limitations. My study relies on expenditure data because of their extensive coverage in most OECD countries over a significant period of time. These data could easily be matched with preferences for changes in spending in corresponding expenditure areas. However, this type of data is known to be vulnerable to not fully capturing the relationship between preferences and policy outcomes. To minimize this limitation, I have extensively discussed whether we can expect a substantial relationship, and have kept only those questions where this assumption is plausible. In particular, there is a large body of research supporting this relationship for social spending, but less evidence for areas such as defense, environmental protection, and public safety. Nevertheless, it is difficult to prove these relationships through statistical tests, so our results depend on these assumptions.

I have chosen to compare changes in the share of GDP because, by using changes in expenditure, it has been possible to differentiate out most of the predictors of expenditure. However, various factors specific to particular countries, issues, or years could influence the rate of change in spending. In order to identify common effects and thus gain substantial knowledge, I have used country-issue and year fixed effects. Caution must be exercised with regard to common errors. To avoid bias, I controlled for economic growth to neutralize the effect of changes in GDP when spending changes as a percentage of GDP, while accounting for common economic shocks during the study period. I also controlled for government ideology as a proxy for past voter preferences, while leaving the effects of term in office uncontrolled due to their importance in our analysis. While I am not aware of any other factors that might influence changes in government spending over time and thereby affect public preferences across countries and issues, it is crucial to recognize that in the real world, there are many potential covariates that have not been considered.

In my analysis of issue areas, I categorized them according to their group relevance and time horizon. Despite striving for straightforward conceptualization and basing our categorization on solid argumentative grounds, the process remains to some extent arbitrary and carries the risk of selection bias. This risk is exacerbated by the relative lack of comprehensive research on specific issues. Nevertheless, I argue that the findings regarding the relevance of issues to young adults are robust. The category was formulated on the basis of strong theoretical arguments based on ideological shifts and self-interest considerations. Concerning time horizons, I conceptualized short- and long-term not based on the typical, but on the potential character of an issue area. This approach enhances objectivity and comparability across contexts. While I am confident that this approach reflects significant aspects of real-world behavior, the finding of the low importance of the time dimension is heavily dependent on these allocations. Hence, I advocate for further critical investigation of this issue. An additional limitation of this study is that, despite the use of spending data, there are only a limited number of observations per issue area. Of the total 314 observations, which include all necessary variables, they span seven issue areas, typically containing 40 to 50 observations each. Thus, the statistical findings concerning different issue areas must be interpreted with caution. While this certainly constitutes a limitation, it further amplifies the explanatory power of the significant findings, thereby reinforcing my arguments.

Despite the inherent limitations, my findings on the low level of substantive representation among young voters are highly significant. By integrating both dynamic and group representation mechanisms, I propose a comprehensive approach that allows for further exploration of my finding - the high dependency on group representation. This exploration is particularly relevant given ongoing demographic shifts that predict an increase in the dependency ratio in favor of the young. Economic welfare and social transfers will increasingly rely on younger populations. At the same time, a noted shift in young adults’ beliefs towards immaterial values and relational equality is increasing the demand for representation of this group, a trend that I expect to grow in the future. My findings underscore the urgency of mechanisms to address these numerical representation gaps and ensure adequate representation in legislative assemblies. Systemic barriers to this progress need to be identified and addressed. Furthermore, it is incumbent upon older policy-makers to promote sensitivity to the demands for equal consideration of the interests of all age groups. This would serve to avoid implicit ideological conflicts and pave the way for a more inclusive democratic system that effectively responds to the diverse needs of its citizens. The research presented here provides a substantial basis from which such progress can be pursued.
