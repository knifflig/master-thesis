Representation in turbulent times

3 Data

The present research seeks to determine the extent to which policy-making in developed democracies is consistent with the preferences of their younger citizens. I focus on changes rather than static levels because I am interested in the link between attitudes to future policy and subsequent policy implementation, both of which relate to changes rather than levels. But the task faces empirical hurdles related to matching robust, consistent measures of public attitudes towards specific policy reforms with measures of subsequent changes in social policy regulation - measures robust enough to support significant causal inferences about representation. Previous studies of influence have attempted to link attitudinal trends among particular groups to the adoption of policies in subsequent years, either by hand-coding policy changes or by using government expenditure data. Both options have drawbacks. While the use of actual policy change is more accurate on the surface, hand-coding those data is subject to several biases such as confirmation bias (Nickerson 1998) or selection bias (Bevan et al. 2013). While usually several strategies are implemented to mitigate these, it’s important to note that no method can completely eliminate bias from human-coded data (de Haas, Natter, and Vezzoli 2015). Furthermore, these data are scarce due to the high level of coding involved and the subsequent need to adapt them to a specific research design. On the other hand, expenditure data are prone to what is known in the literature as the “dependent variable problem” (Alesina and Barro 2001; Siegel 2007), which refers to the problem that expenditure indicators never exclusively explain actual legislative changes. Esping-Andersen (1990) noted that expenditure, especially when measured as a share of GDP, can provide a potentially misleading picture of policy differences. The main problem lies in the inherent difficulty of distinguishing whether a chosen change is the result of responsive policies or of factors beyond national control. As Allan and Scruggs (2004) point out, the ratio of expenditure to GDP can be misleading, especially in times of economic recession when the denominator of the measure falls. Although a nominal focus on changes in spending is ambiguous, it is a valuable source of information as it indicates if respondents are met. As long as the government’s main policy instruments are involved with increasing or decreasing spending in that area I can capture the responsiveness to that preferences adequately. Thus, I must critically put in question in which areas, changes in public spending actually reflect the policy change. Brooks and Manza (2006a) have argued that government officials are particularly responsible for the redistribution of wealth and should therefore be responsive to public preferences regarding the welfare state, resulting in what they call Welfare State Effort. The rich political economy literature has tested this link, particularly in the context of welfare spending, and has provided robust evidence for the correlation between preferences and welfare state spending in advanced democracies (Esping-Andersen 1990; Burstein 2003; Brooks and Manza 2006b; Soroka and Wlezien 2009; Wlezien and Soroka 2009).

3.1 Policy output

We address these empirical challenges by matching changes in public spending in specific issue areas with the preferences expressed by the population for spending changes in those same areas. This task is accomplished by matching data on government spending in specific key areas provided by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) with data from the International Social Survey Programme (ISSP). It is therefore reasonable to expect that differences in social spending across countries and time periods are likely to be of explanatory importance. The ISSP data covers preferences towards spending in nine areas: total spending, the environment; health; the police and law enforcement; education; the military and defense; old age pensions; unemployment benefits; culture/arts. The government expenditure database by the OECD (OECD 2023) provides a breakdown of government expenditure according to the Classification of the Functions of Government (COFOG) from the European system of accounts (Eurostat 2013). For the purposes of this study, I use data from the OECD because of its inherent advantages. OECD’s standardized, transparent data collection methods, coupled with the homogeneity of its member countries in terms of democratic practices and administrative capacity (Wilensky 2002), significantly reduce the likelihood of bias. This does not mean, however, that all OECD countries are perfectly homogeneous. Differences remain in political, social, and economic contexts, including public policies, social structures, and political institutions. For instance, a Nordic welfare state like Sweden is very different from a more liberal market economy like the US, although both are OECD countries. It’s also important to note that the use of OECD data alone may be subject to selection bias if the research question extends beyond the OECD context. In particular, the generalisability of my findings beyond the OECD context is limited because OECD countries are predominantly high-income countries and therefore do not adequately represent the global community. Therefore, the interpretation of my results within this specific context will be of critical importance.

Figures 3.1 and 3.2 show the included types of services. These records cover several OECD countries and extend from 1995 to 2022. However, my investigation is limited to a specific period, 1996 to 2016, for the purpose of synchronization with data from the ISSP, while the use of expenditure data is extended to 2020 to allow for the lagged effects of policy implementation. The surveys conducted by the ISSP during this period provide valuable data that complement my analysis. Consequently, the temporal limitation is necessitated by the available surveys conducted in 1996, 2006, and 2016. Our empirical analysis focuses on 25 countries for which I have data for at least one point in time within the chosen timeframe. These countries are Austria, Belgium, Chile, Czech Republic, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Hungary, Iceland, Ireland, Israel, Japan, Korea, Netherlands, Norway, Poland, Portugal, Slovakia, Slovenia, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, United Kingdom, and the United States. If respondents express a preference for increased spending, and this is followed by an actual increase in spending in the area in question in subsequent years, this is interpreted as evidence of responsiveness for the group in question. Similarly, if a preference for a reduction is followed by a decrease in spending in subsequent years, this is also considered to be evidence of responsiveness. As the political process often takes some time, I have to take into account the lagged effects of policy changes. To measure changes in spending over the four years following the survey, I follow the widely used moving average approach (e.g., Soroka and Wlezien 2005; Kymlicka and Banting 2006; Schakel, Burgoon, and Hakhverdian 2020). It is important to note that my research uses country-year-issue as the primary unit of analysis. Therefore, the total number of cases can be interpreted from this perspective. In theory, my indicator ranges from 0 to 100 and symbolizes a percentage of the country’s gross domestic product (GDP), calculated in local currency with 2015 as the base year.

Figure 3.1: Government expenditure by issue area

Looking at the data, Figure 3.1 shows total annual government expenditure. The years 1996, 2006, and 2016, represented by the vertical lines, are the years in which the preferences were measured. These preferences were then compared with the changes in expenditure over the following four years. The average government expenditure in the countries studied was around 45 percent of GDP, although in some cases it exceeded 60 percent of GDP. Total government expenditure was significantly higher in the mid-1990s and after the 2008 financial crisis. Thereafter, however, a steady decline was observed until a sudden increase in 2016. In particular, the change in expenditure in 2016 is indicative of the fluctuations in the following four years up to 2020. The observed trend is important because it provides valuable insight into the distribution of changing expenditures. Moreover, the trend may already indicate that developed democracies are facing a shrinking tax base, which puts more pressure on the prioritization of spending and, under normal circumstances, on the need to reduce overall spending. However, the rapid increases after 2008 and 2015 may be related to external factors. Looking more closely at the different areas of expenditure, the highest shares of total expenditure were allocated to pensions, education, and health. These are the main drivers of social expenditure. Conversely, unemployment, the last welfare factor measured in this study, accounted for around 1 percent on average, with considerable variation between countries. Expenditure on pensions also varied considerably, with some countries spending as much as 15 percent of GDP on pensions. Expenditure on defense and public security, on the other hand, was more consistent across countries at around 2 percent. A notable outlier is Israel, which spends almost 8 percent of its GDP on defense. In contrast, expenditure on cultural services and environmental protection remained low, averaging less than 1 percent.

Figure 3.2: Change in government expenditure by issue area

The change in spending averaged over the subsequent four years is presented in Figure 3.2. We can see that government spending on different categories of expenditure has varied considerably. Starting from 1996, health, education, old age, and unemployment have all experienced changes in expenditure, with a particularly high variance in expenditure for unemployment, which has a standard deviation (SD) of 9.62. In the late 1990s, we see a trend of increased spending on health and education. For instance, in 1997, health expenditure changed by 1.75 percent, while education spending experienced a minor change of 0.0139 percent. At the same time, spending on pensions was slightly reduced, and unemployment saw a substantial reduction, with a change of -5.14 percent. Around the 2000s, spending across all areas notably fluctuated. A dramatic increase in unemployment spending was observed in 2005 and 2006, reaching a change of 16 and 17.7 percent respectively, with very high variances (647 and 628 respectively), reflecting a significant disparity in how countries dealt with unemployment during that period. After the financial crisis in 2008, while spending on health, education, and old age saw relative stability, unemployment spending again varied widely. For instance, in 2010, unemployment spending had a change of -4.55 percent, demonstrating the extreme spending changes countries made in response to unemployment issues during this period. In recent years, specifically in 2016, health saw a substantial increase in spending change (4.29 percent). This coincided with a considerable rise in spending change in unemployment (19.7), with a high SD of 21.8 and variance of 476, pointing to a broad disparity in how different countries addressed unemployment issues. defense spending experienced a major decline in the late 1990s with the largest fall observed in 1996, with an average decrease of -3.21 percent. There was a period of rebound in the early 2000s, with a slight dip in 2001, but from 2002 onwards, there is a noticeable drop, bottoming in 2010 with an average decrease of -3.62 percent. However, defense spending increased dramatically in 2015 and 2016, reaching a significant peak of 7.04 percent change in 2016. Public order and safety spending, on the other hand, showed a generally steadier trend over the years. There was a small decrease in 1996 (-0.197 percent) but the expenditure level steadily rose afterwards. The years 2005 and 2016 saw the most substantial increases, with 1.92 percent and 1.70 percent respectively. For environmental protection spending, there is a clear upward trend starting from 1996. It reached its peak in 2001 with an impressive increase of 6.40 percent. However, it started declining in 2012 and reached a low of -2.00 percent in 2012. Afterward, there was a gradual recovery and the spending increased by 3.19 percent in 2016. Cultural services spending also saw a varied trajectory, with a noticeable increase in 1998 (2.77 percent) and a sharp fall in 2009 and 2011. However, spending recovered in the subsequent years and reached an increase of 1.98 percent in 2016.

Looking at the spending levels between countries there is considerable variation, which contributes positively to measures of responsiveness, as it allows to observe patterns and changes in spending, which can then be compared with changes in public opinion data. It is important to emphasize that social welfare expenditure represents a large proportion of total government expenditure. As such, changes in this area can have a significant impact on the government’s budget and a major impact on the well-being of the population. However, not all expenditure areas show rapid change. Some areas, such as the environment, defense, and pensions, tend to show slower changes in spending. This slower pace may be due to the inherent nature of these sectors. For example, environmental policies or defense strategies tend to require long-term planning and cannot be changed abruptly without potential disruption. Possible reasons for these differences in speed could be related to the short or long-term effects of policy changes. In areas such as unemployment, policy changes may have immediate and visible effects, which could lead to faster spending responses. On the other hand, policy changes in areas such as environment or defense may have more delayed effects, leading to slower spending adjustments. In addition, changes in pension policy tend to be slower due to the long-term nature of these benefits and the need for stability and predictability for beneficiaries. Abrupt changes can disrupt the livelihoods of a significant proportion of the population, especially the elderly, so governments could be inclined to take a more gradual approach in these areas. In the next section, I take a closer look at the measures of public preferences before discussing whether and how I can reasonably match these variables.

3.2 Public preferences

Citizen attitudes are gathered from the multi-country International Social Science Programme (ISSP), conducted in 25 OECD countries in 1996 (ISSP Research Group 1999), 2006 (ISSP Research Group 2021) and 2016 (ISSP Research Group 2018). The ISSP’s repeated Role of Government module provides valuable insights into public preferences for increasing or decreasing government spending in these areas, and allows this research to reliably quantify support for such government expenditure across a wide range of countries and years. The available data provided by the ISSP cover preferences towards spending in nine areas: total spending, the environment; health; the police and law enforcement; education; the military and defense; old age pensions; unemployment benefits; culture and arts. To ensure homogeneity, the response categories have been systematically restructured: strong agreement with the increase in spending is given a score of 100, simple agreement is given 50, neutral is given 0, disagreement is given -50, and strong disagreement is given -100. These data cover a relatively long period of time, in most OECD countries, and a wide range of issues. The questions in the Role of Government module have multiple advantages. They have been carried out in a similar way three times in 20 years and shed light on trends in preferences for the welfare state and a range of democratic functions in developed democracies on which citizens might be expected to have an opinion. The questions are designed to capture respondents’ views on the expansion or contraction of spending, thereby measuring shifts proportional to the existing size of the program under consideration. Although these questions nominally focus on expenditure, the implication of a change in expenditure indicates a change in program dimensions. However, such results may also be the result of a particular reallocation of resources or a modification of the organizational structure. As a result, respondents are not expected to perform fiscal accounting, but primarily to articulate how they feel about future outcomes.

The challenge of studying youth political representation begins with an inherently definitional problem: defining who qualifies as young is less definitive than other socio-political categories, such as gender or ethnicity. In the academic discourse on young adults, researchers often operationalize young adults as individuals under the age of 30, 35, or 40 (Stockemer and Sundström 2021; Joshi 2013; Belschner and Garcia de Paredes 2020), following the advice of the Interparlamentarion Union (Union 2016). In particular, a demarcation at the age of 40 has advantages when studying descriptive representation due to prevailing institutional constraints: some legislative bodies enforce high minimum age requirements for office, and it is rare to observe individuals securing office before the age of 35 (Belschner and Garcia de Paredes 2020; Union 2016). However, as the present study is concerned mainly with substantive representation, it is less constrained by such institutional limitations. Given an approximate average life expectancy of roughly 80 years during the period under study (Harman 2006), the 40-year threshold includes half of that life expectancy, making it implausible. The choice of an age cut-off necessarily involves a degree of arbitrariness; thus, this study uses the commonly used cut-off of 35 years. I postulate that any significant effect observed at this level should be equal or stronger if the cut-off is lowered to 30 years, thus providing a more convincing argument than a significant finding that might not persist with a higher cut-off.

As argued, the issue of uncrystallized substantive interests is inextricably linked to the demand for substantive representation, which leads us to question the age at which this demand emerges. The age of eligibility to vote is generally 18, while some countries are discussing or have already implemented a voting age of 16 (Rosenqvist 2017). This might be a plausible threshold, as it symbolizes the transition to formal political participation. However, this methodologically convenient cut-off point leads to several complications: my research also seeks to capture non-conventional forms of political expression that are not subject to formal age restrictions; political interests do not emerge spontaneously upon reaching voting age, but are usually present earlier, as evidenced by discussions on lowering the voting age (Zeglovits and Aichholzer 2014); and the implementation of different age cut-off points across my sample may introduce bias due to unequal representation. With these considerations in mind, my study examines the substantive representation of people aged between 15 and 34. This strategy allows us to capture the political effects of those who are currently eligible to vote, or who will be eligible to vote within the next three years, without introducing bias by excluding a significant non-voting cohort. Therefore, in the context of this study, I use the terms “young adults”, “young people”, “the youth”, “young voters” interchangeably to refer to individuals in this age.

Figure 3.3: Average preferences towards spending in different areas per year and age group

Since the 1970s numerous empirical studies have shown evidence for a generational shift from material needs to post-materialist values, as societies become more affluent and secure. In many contexts, younger and older generations have distinct preferences regarding how politicians should distribute limited government resources: Younger generations tend to favor spending on quality of life and equality (Abramson and Inglehart 1995; Kitschelt 1988; Lichtin, van der Brug, and Rekker 2023) and especially support environmental protection (Gough et al. 2008). Older generations in contrast show greater support for military spending (Wattenberg 2020). However in some areas like welfare state, generational differences are under question, especially if they are driven by self-interest (Ebbinghaus and Naumann 2017; Greer et al. 2021). Older citizens, typically prefer greater spending on public pensions and healthcare(Sihvo and Uusitalo 1995; Goerres 2009; Naumann 2017), while young adults are more inclined with education (Busemeyer, Goerres, and Weschle 2009), which both disproportionately benefit the respective age group.

Figure 3.3 provides a comprehensive analysis of public opinion on government spending, the data in this study describe the preferences of citizens under the age of 35 compared to their older counterparts for spending changes, averaged across all countries surveyed, by issue and by year. One striking pattern that emerges from the data is an increased value placed on education by younger demographic groups, a finding that confirms previous research. This preference has been consistent over time, with the percentage of younger demographic groups supporting an increase in education spending measured at 48 percent in 1996, 52.8 percent in 2006, and 52.6 percent in 2016. Cultural services and environmental protection show similar trends, as might be expected given their strong correlation with non-material issues. The younger population has shown constant support for environmental protection, with support levels of 38.8 against 28.9 in 1996, 35.8 against 29.5 in 2006, and 31.8 against 25.6 in 2016. While this support has declined over time, it remains stronger than that of older generations. Younger citizens also show a tendency to support increases in unemployment benefits, although this effect is subject to considerable fluctuations over time. Meanwhile, older people have shown more robust support for spending on pensions, consistently outstripping younger people. A similar trend can be seen for public safety, possibly reflecting generational differences in societal perceptions or increasing safety demands with age. However, health spending shows minimal variation, suggesting that the equity demands of younger generations may coincide with the self-interest of older citizens who rely heavily on health services. Although both age groups generally express opposition to increased defense spending, as indicated by negative scores, this opposition appears to diminish over time. Specifically, the older population’s score gradually increases from -17.8 in 1996 to 5.52 in 2016, suggesting a shift from opposition to support. This finding, although interesting, is beyond the scope of my study.

In addition, it is worth noting that public sentiment towards government spending as a whole has declined significantly over the two decades covered by these data. Notwithstanding the persistently negative sentiment towards government spending as a whole, there is a trend that suggests a shift towards a more favorable view. This apparent contradiction could be explained by several factors. First, the divergence may be due to the fiscal illusion that taxpayers often underestimate the true cost of goods and services provided by the government, leading to a preference for increased government spending in certain areas. However, when asked about overall government spending, respondents may be considering the wider implications of such spending, such as higher taxes or larger budget deficits, hence their overall negative sentiment (Dollery and Worthington 1996). Second, it’s important to recognize that public preferences are not monolithic, but rather the aggregation of individual preferences. When respondents express a preference for increased spending in a particular area, they may be indicating a desire for a shift in budgetary priorities rather than an overall increase in spending. Thus, when asked about overall spending, they may still be expressing a negative sentiment if they believe that current levels are excessive or poorly allocated (Bremer and Bürgisser 2022). Third, the phenomenon may be due to a perceived disconnect between government spending and the outcomes it produces. Individuals may have reservations about the overall efficiency or effectiveness of government spending [as already discussed in the context of long-term commitments (Alan M. Jacobs 2011; Alan M. Jacobs 2016, to name just one possible explanation). These concerns could lead to negative attitudes towards overall spending, even if they support increased funding in areas that directly affect their lives. The contradiction between preferences for increased spending in specific areas and opposition to overall government spending thus underlines the need to ask what my measurement is about. This research uses data that ask about preferences for more or less spending on particular issues, which has some drawbacks. I am aware that opinions expressed are not so much about the spending itself as about the implied consequences. Therefore, for each issue area, it has been critically asked whether it is plausible to reconcile the measured preferences with government expenditure data. The data analyzed provides tangible evidence of clear differences in most of the areas covered. I suggest that younger adults are more supportive of education, cultural services, and environmental protection. Conversely, older citizens are more supportive of pensions, public safety, and defense. Unemployment benefits and health are not clearly allocated. The following section will take a closer look at each issue.

3.3 Issue areas

Our research uses data that ask about preferences for more or less spending on particular issues, which has some drawbacks. Therefore, for each issue area, I discuss three major issues at stake: (1) What are respondents intended preferences when they express their opinion towards more or less spending? (2) Can we expect to see responsiveness reflected in changed spending? (3) What political trade-offs do policymakers face when making spending decisions?

3.3.1 Overall government spending

In order to understand preferences regarding government spending, it is crucial to consider what respondents have in mind when they express their views. In terms of overall public spending, it is possible that a tendency to increase spending is linked to a desire for a wider range of public services, better infrastructure, or a possible expectation of economic stimulus. Conversely, a tendency towards lower spending could represent a desire for fiscal austerity, a more compact government structure, or a reduction in taxation. Given the wide range of policy areas that total government spending can cover, it remains a challenge to decipher a definitive pattern of responsiveness in spending changes that reflects these preferences. I have therefore chosen to exclude them from my subsequent examination.

3.3.2 Education

Preferences for spending on education show strong support for an increase across society, but it is particularly pronounced among younger demographic groups, as evidenced by the research conducted by Busemeyer, Goerres, and Weschle (2009) and reinforced by my findings derived from the International Social Survey Programme (ISSP) data. In the context of advanced democracies, it is reasonable to assume that most respondents understand the concept of education, albeit with ambiguity about the implications of increased spending on it. Consequently, I suggest that this inclination towards increased spending largely reflects a desire for improved quality of education in relation to respondents’ perceived evaluation of the existing education system.2 Policymakers have a wide range of policy tools at their disposal when formulating decisions on education spending. Although there are a variety of strategies for implementing policies that affect daily life, such as changes in school or university regulations, I argue that the desired quality of education is primarily financed through teacher competence. Most spending is therefore catalyzed by the imperative need for a highly skilled workforce. As such, improvements in educational standards will have a strong correlation with expenditure. Increasing the size or skill level of the workforce requires significant structural changes, which typically have a long-term time horizon with results that may take several years or even decades to manifest. As a result, I expect changes in spending to accurately reflect policymakers’ long-term fiscal decisions, with education being of primary importance for the younger population. For example, research by Bertocchi et al. (2020) suggests that a one percent increase in youth voter turnout leads to a 0.77 percent increase in higher education spending. This finding reinforces my expectation in two ways: first, it highlights the unique importance of this sector for young adults, and second, it underscores the importance of conventional political participation, which is typically associated with issue areas driven by long-term spending decisions.

3.3.3 Pensions

Preferences for increased spending on pensions tend to be more pronounced among older population groups, a trend confirmed by several academic studies (Sihvo and Uusitalo 1995; Goerres 2009; Naumann 2017). By analogy with the education scenario, it can be concluded that the propensity to escalate spending is largely driven by the pursuit of enhanced pension benefits. In navigating the complex landscape of pension spending decisions, a key challenge is to strike a balance between meeting the immediate needs of current pensioners and ensuring the long-term sustainability of the system for future generations. The policy mechanisms that affect pension levels range from changes in the retirement age to revisions in the benefit formula and adjustments in contribution rates. However, these different approaches share a common theme: they determine both the number of beneficiaries and the level of their benefits, which in turn leads to variations in total expenditure. Increasing pension benefits requires a larger pool of resources, which increases expenditure. Consequently, a preference for higher spending is inherently linked to a preference for higher pension benefits. Given the significant direct impact of pensions on the lives of older adults, this area takes on immense importance. For example, Goerres (2009) shows that pension-related issues are often strong motivators for political engagement among older adults. Although (national) pension systems typically do not involve direct payments from the government but rather represent large transfer accounts (OECD 2017), any changes to pensions inherently take a long-term perspective. This is due to the fact that the full impact of reform measures often only becomes apparent over a longer period of time.

3.3.4 Health

In my study of demographic preferences for government spending, I found an overarching preference for increased spending across all age groups, with no clear preference for either the young or the old. This observation differs from what might be expected given the tendency of older populations to require more regular and intensive medical services, suggesting a potential for greater policy responsiveness to this demographic group in health-related matters (Sihvo and Uusitalo 1995; Goerres 2009; Naumann 2017). However, this assumption could be balanced by the tendency of younger generations to advocate spending that improves the quality of life and promotes equality (Abramson and Inglehart 1995; Kitschelt 1988; Lichtin, van der Brug, and Rekker 2023), principles that are intrinsically linked to the concept of national health insurance. This dichotomy presents two potentially conflicting perspectives on the issue: the first emphasizes improving the overall quality of health care, while the second advocates greater equality. The drive for greater equity would require the expansion of national insurance, which like improving quality, would be reflected in changes in spending patterns. This nuanced landscape of preferences and spending trends, which vary across countries and over time, provides an invaluable lens through which to examine the primary motivations behind policy decisions. While legislators generally have no incentive to promote the interests of their specific demographic groups, examining whether spending allocations are more closely aligned with the preferences of the young or the old can provide insights into these primary drivers. In the health sector, government spending is mainly targeted at health infrastructure, services, public health initiatives, and research, and includes activities such as hospital funding, drug subsidies, prevention programs, and investment in medical research. Some of these efforts, such as infrastructure development, are long-term investments, but I argue that there are many ways in which government budgets can be used for immediate improvements. For example, a significant proportion of health care is dependent on the level of national insurance; changes to this will have an immediate impact on both public perception and government spending.

3.3.5 Unemployment

Our examination of public attitudes to unemployment spending across age cohorts reveals a common preference to increase spending, albeit to a much lesser extent than other welfare state concerns examined. The data suggest that the younger population is slightly more supportive of increasing such spending, a trend that was marginally evident in 2006 but significant in other years. In contrast, older demographic groups appear to be more resistant to policy interventions aimed at reducing income inequality at least in the United States (Wattenberg 2020). The dataset, which comes from advanced democracies, allows to conclude that respondents generally have a good understanding of the concept of unemployment. As a result, I postulate that an inclination towards increased spending essentially symbolizes a desire for more comprehensive unemployment insurance coverage and dynamic labor market policies aimed at reducing unemployment rates, a factor that should be reflected in changes in spending. Policy decisions on unemployment spending cover a range of different instruments, like unemployment insurance schemes, active labor market policies such as retraining programs, and structural changes to support labor demand. Each of these instruments has different implementation times and degrees of effectiveness. For example, changes to unemployment insurance benefits can be implemented quickly and have an immediate impact on beneficiaries, thus being classified as short-term initiatives. This observation is consistent with a significant fluctuation in changes in unemployment benefits in my OECD data, coupled with considerable heterogeneity in the way different countries deal with unemployment. On the other hand, active labor market policies and structural changes, including public investment in emerging industries, require longer timeframes and produce tangible results only in the medium to long term. But as in the case of health services, there is a wealth of opportunities to implement policies with immediate visibility, such as increasing financial subsidies.

3.3.6 Defense

In conceptualizing voters’ preferences for an increase in defense spending, I hypothesize that the focus is not on the direct impact on unemployment benefits, but is primarily driven by ethical considerations. Given that no OECD country was involved in territorial conflicts during the period under study, I suggest that the vast majority of preferences expressed by respondents are based on normative judgments (see for example, Rudolph, Freitag, and Thurner 2022). This hypothesis explains two observations from my data. First, the aftermath of the Cold War and the emergence of extensive peace movements in the 1980s understandably correlate with a prominent majority in favor of reduced military spending in the 1990s, an effect that dissipates over time. Second, the generational shift towards a more egalitarian stance that embraces transnational cooperation as opposed to nationalist protectionism, correlates with even stronger opposition to defense spending, although this trend is not uniformly evident in the 2016 survey. This theory is supported by data showing increased support for military spending among older generations (Wattenberg 2020), suggesting greater relevance within this demographic. However, it could be argued that the complexity of defense issues and the influence of various non-age-specific factors, such as geopolitical considerations and defense industry lobbying, may limit the extent to which policymakers can advocate for their social groups. Nevertheless, I argue that a predominant proportion of MPs will defend normative values similar to those of their contemporaries, thereby demonstrating significant responsiveness. This is partly supported by the descriptive data. The late 1990s saw a significant decline in defense spending, with the steepest fall in 1996 when the average decrease was -3.21 percent. There was a recovery in the early 2000s, albeit with a slight dip in 2001. However, since 2002 there has been a noticeable decline, reaching its lowest point in 2010 with an average fall of -3.62 percent. Conversely, there was a dramatic increase in defense spending in 2015 and 2016, culminating in a significant peak with a change of 7.04 percent in 2016. This illustrates the societal shift from a position of opposition to increased spending to one of mild support. Setting defense budgets is a complex exercise involving political trade-offs. Policymakers must balance the need to maintain an extensive defense establishment with other fiscal priorities. Defense strategies often require investments in infrastructure, equipment procurement, personnel training, and multi-year procurement contracts, making these decisions somewhat impervious to short-term shifts in public sentiment. Moreover, the expected outcomes of defense spending, such as enhanced national security, are not immediately apparent to the public, making it difficult for voters to relate these decisions to their immediate outcomes. However, the majority of decisions are likely to revolve around the procurement of equipment or the payment of salaries, which I suggest will maintain a robust correlation with spending data.

3.3.7 Public safety

Public opinion on the allocation of resources to public safety initiatives can be interpreted as a reflection of societal sentiment on the need for enhanced security measures, increased law enforcement resources, and comprehensive crime prevention strategies. Perceptions of safety and security are inherently subjective and can be influenced by various factors such as age, geographical location, and personal experience. Younger demographic groups tend to be more concerned about issues such as digital security, while their older counterparts may be more concerned about physical security and property crime. The emphasis on increasing police presence can be perceived as a conservative viewpoint and tends to be associated predominantly with older demographics, a pattern that is consistently supported by previous research, including that conducted by Wattenberg (2020). In the context of fiscal changes related to public safety, it can be hypothesized that increases in public safety spending should generally be accompanied by advances in public safety provision. These advances could take the form of increased manpower or upgraded equipment, or they could result from changes in the regulatory framework, which may not necessarily be reflected in budgetary adjustments. However, it is important to bear in mind that shifts in expenditure do not automatically signal policy responsiveness, especially considering the possibility of false negatives, where substantive policy changes may not be accurately captured in the fiscal data. This notion could explain the observed stability of public order and security spending over time, as shown by my dataset. A marginal decrease of 0.197 percent was recorded in 1996, but this was followed by a steady increase in expenditure. In particular, the years 2005 and 2016 were marked by significant budget expansions of 1.92 percent and 1.70 percent respectively. Issues related to public safety have an immediate and tangible impact on the lives of citizens, making them a crucial focus in the short-term policy arena. Most actions taken to improve public safety are quickly visible, and any shortcomings are equally quickly identified, giving this policy area a heightened political sensitivity.

3.3.8 Environmental Protection

Looking at the issue of environmental protection, there is a broad societal consensus in favor of increased spending, although younger people are more inclined to do so. However, this diverse contextual scope introduces potential discrepancies in the interpretation of environmentalism. The prevailing literature suggests a discernible trend of shifting concern from local environmental issues to broader climate change policy (Dietz, Dan, and Shwom 2007). Our data, which is consistent with this view, suggests that this shift may lead to a decline in support (Kenny 2017). Research in this area reveals substantive links between environmentalism and intangible, left-wing, and libertarian values, touching on both local environmental and climate change issues (Inglehart 1977; Kriesi 2010; Gullberg and Aardal 2018). These ideological leanings are strikingly consistent with younger cohorts (Abramson and Inglehart 1995; Gough et al. 2008). When addressing environmental protection through fiscal policy, legislators have a diverse arsenal of strategies at their disposal. Options range from direct commitments, such as funding renewable energy initiatives, to indirect incentives, such as tax breaks for environmentally friendly practices. As in the case of defense, achieving significant improvements in environmental protection depends primarily on substantial investment, often at the expense of short-term welfare state policies (Schulze 2021). This fiscal allocation is necessitated by the integral technological innovation and infrastructure development required. The effects of environmental policies typically unfold over long periods of time. The full impact of current decisions may not be felt for many years or even decades. Consequently, I suggest that the high importance attached to environmental protection by younger populations will not elicit a strong level of responsiveness, as it contradicts the views of a large proportion of older MPs. Furthermore, I expect changes in environmental spending to reflect the long-term strategic considerations of policymakers. These should show comparable responsiveness across the age spectrum, as the extended time horizon serves to dilute the disadvantage of low participation rates among the younger population while creating opportunities to show responsiveness to this group.

3.3.9 Culture

Analysis of the ISSP data reveals a discernible trend: younger cohorts show an increased interest to increase cultural expenditure, a sentiment not shared by their older counterparts. Understanding the implicit meanings behind expressed preferences for increased or decreased cultural spending is a complex task. Such expenditure can be allocated in a variety of ways, from subsidizing local arts events to investing in the preservation and maintenance of national heritage sites. As such, the tendency to increase cultural spending may reflect a deeper yearning for a richer, more accessible cultural milieu in one’s immediate environment. Nevertheless, the heterogeneity of preferences can vary considerably across different age groups, potentially leading to a wide range of interpretations of the issue. The diversity inherent in the ways in which cultural funding is allocated poses a challenge to the claim that an increase in government spending would to some extent reflect the preferences expressed by different age groups. Consequently, these factors are not included in the parameters of my analysis due to their unreliable correlation with expressed preferences.

3.4 Control variables

The focus of my research is on the variable of interest, namely the change in government expenditure over a four-year period following a survey. All country or issue-specific time-invariant predictors influencing government expenditure remain constant and are therefore not subject to discernible changes over time. However, it is crucial to recognize the factors that simultaneously influence public preferences and the evolution of government spending. In an effort to account for this relationship, I have included three control variables in my model. The first variable takes into account the policies implemented. The origin of these policies is strongly linked to the ideological convictions of the parties in power (Brooks and Manza 2006a; Binzer Hobolt and Klemmensen 2007). At the same time, political parties in power can be seen as the expression of public preferences through the democratic process of voting. This paradigm is succinctly captured in Mansbridge (2003) concept of promissory representation. To ensure an accurate cross-country comparison over time, I account for this potential confounding factor by incorporating the ideological position of the ruling government into my model. Instead of using the commonly used left-right political spectrum, I take a novel approach by measuring the frequency of positive references to non-economic demographic sectors in party manifestos. This data comes from the Party Manifesto Project (Lehmann et al. 2023), with a particular focus on women, students, and age groups. This method offers a unique advantage: it quantifies the ideological predispositions of governing parties towards specific demographic groups, which is more in line with my variables of interest than the traditional left-right spectrum. The ideological positions of parties are calculated individually and then averaged on the basis of the number of parties in government. While an alternative approach would be to average positions according to the distribution of parliamentary seats, such a method neglects the consensus-driven nature of coalition governments (Sabatier and Weible 2007). I have therefore opted for the former method. Additionally, to capture changes in government I use the average position in the four years after the survey. The distribution is presented in figure 3.5. The maximum ranges up to 10 mentions on average in 1996 and declines afterward. in 2016 each party in government have between 0 and 2.5 mentions in their manifesto.

Figure 3.5: Distribution of gov. ideology and GDB growth

The change in government expenditure is measured by the change in government expenditure as a share of GDP. This method of calculation provides an efficient, relative, and normalized understanding of government spending and serves as an indicator of government priorities over time. Particularly In this context of demographic changes, fluctuations in government expenditure as a share of GDP provide a critical measure of the relative importance that the government attaches to different sectors. A second key advantage of this measurement strategy is its ability to normalize government expenditure over different time periods, irrespective of economic conditions or size. This helps to maintain the comparability of government spending over time, thus overcoming the methodological challenge of variable economic performance. Furthermore, this method has the potential to disentangle whether variations in the relative measure of government expenditure are the result of deliberate policy choices or merely an arithmetic by-product of economic fluctuations. As a result, this approach increases the sensitivity of the measure to policy variations, but to avoid measuring the GDP growth itself I additionally included GDP growth (Kang and Powell 2010; Wlezien and Soroka 2012) in the model. In this framework, a relative change in expenditure could be due to an actual change in expenditure, but it could also reflect fluctuations in GDP. For example, if expenditure policy remains stable but GDP increases, the share of expenditure will automatically appear smaller due to the increase in the denominator. As a result, GDP growth is expected to have a negative impact if the policy landscape remains unchanged. Given that GDP growth has a uniform impact across all expenditure sectors, any observed differences in impact, ranging from slightly negative to other magnitudes, are likely to be due to changes in actual spending. This underlines the importance of including GDP growth in the model to disentangle the effects of economic changes from deliberate policy choices. I again use the four years average. To account for the asymmetric size of the age groups, I additionally include the ratio of young adults to the total population, derived from the OECD data sets, as a control variable in all models with separate age group effects. I go on to discuss the structural approach to my data.

3.4.1 Cross validating data matching

When matching different data sources, it is essential to validate and confirm the data used (Shimodaira 2016). This highlights the need to cross-check the number of respondents per age group from the International Social Survey Programme (ISSP) data with the size of the corresponding age group in a given country from the OECD data. There are two reasons for this validation process. First, the ISSP dataset is a collection of cross-national collaborations on topics important to social science research, such as the role of government, social networks, and social inequality. However, as the number of respondents per age group in the ISSP data may not fully capture the diversity or size of each age group in each country, cross-checking with OECD data helps to ensure that the representation is proportionate and reflects the demographics of each country. Secondly, the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) provides reliable, high-quality, and comparable data on a wide range of topics, including population demographics. Consequently, comparing ISSP data with OECD data provides a more accurate and comprehensive understanding of the size and distribution of different age groups in a country. Furthermore, this comparison is crucial for two reasons. It allows us to determine whether the sample of respondents in the ISSP data is representative of the country’s demographics as captured by the OECD data. It also ensures that any public preference expressed in the ISSP data can be accurately generalized to the corresponding age group in the population. Such verification can therefore help to remove bias from the data and improve the robustness of subsequent analysis and findings.

Figure 3.6: Percentage of population in OECD data and on ISSP respondents

Figure 3.6 shows the proportions of different age groups per year from both datasets. Let’s look first at the old age group. In 1996, the proportion of the ‘old’ age group in the sample is about 68.5 percent(995 out of 1453), while in the population it is 50.8 percent. Similarly, in 2006 it is about 75.2 percent in the sample (1050 out of 1396) compared to 55.2 percent in the population. In 2016, the figures are about 78.5 percent in the sample (1017 out of 1295) and 58.0 percent in the population. Looking at these figures, it’s clear that the old age group is over-represented in the sample in all three years compared to their actual proportions in the population. Let’s look at the young age group the other way around. In 1996, the share of the ‘young’ age group in the sample is about 31.5 percent(458 out of 1453), while in the population it is 29.4 percent. In 2006, it was about 24.8 percent in the sample (347 out of 1396) compared to 27.6 percent in the population. In 2016, the figures are around 21.5 percent in the sample (278 out of 1295) and 25.1 percent in the population. This analysis shows that the young age group is fairly well represented in 1996, but tends to be slightly under-represented in the sample for the years 2006 and 2016, compared to their actual proportions in the population. In summary, while the ‘young’ age group is fairly well represented in 1996, there appears to be a general over-representation of the old age group and an under-representation of the young age group across all years when compared with the country’s demographic data from the OECD. This indicates a bias in the representation of age groups, which should be taken into account when interpreting results derived from these data.

Figure 3.7: Probability of a mismatch between population and respondents

To account for the asymmetric size of the age groups, I include the ratio of young adults to the total population as a control variable. However, this integration of population proportions does not in itself correct the skewness in the representation of age cohorts in the respondent data collected in the ISSP surveys. This is due to the fact that while the inclusion of population proportions helps to gain a broader understanding of the structure of society and its potential influence on the results obtained, it does not correct for the potential bias caused by the uneven representation of different age groups within the survey data. A commonly used statistical tool, the chi-square test, is used to determine the presence of a significant association between categorical variables (Steiger 1990). In the current scenario, it is used to determine whether there is a significant discrepancy between the observed age group distribution in the ISSP data and the expected distribution based on OECD data. If the calculated p-value is less than the chosen significance threshold, the null hypothesis of equivalence between the observed and expected distributions is rejected, and it is concluded that there is a significant difference in the age group distribution within the sampled data compared to the actual population. This analysis is visually presented in Figure 3.7. All the data from Latvia and Lithuania that were found to be unrepresentative raise serious questions about the data selection methods used in these countries, leading to their exclusion. Other questionable observations come mainly from the 1996 sample, possibly due to the different methodological standards of the time. However, the categorical exclusion of all observations exceeding a p-value of 0.05 may not be the optimal strategy for dealing with imbalances in age group representation within your data. Relying solely on the result of a chi-square test to exclude observations could lead to data distortion and potential bias, potentially removing valuable information that is integral to other facets of the analysis. To mitigate this, I undertake a comprehensive examination of the distribution of the 63 observations where the p-value exceeds the 1 percent significance threshold. As other methods, such as weighting or oversampling, or undersampling, have problems with increased variance of estimates and overfitting, I exclude 39 observations that exceed the 10 percent significance level. These are mainly from the 1996 survey data. In addition, I ran my analysis at several other levels to check robustness.

3.5 Outliers

Figure 3.8: Cook distance of all observations with cutoff 3 in the main model

Exceptionally divergent data points, often referred to as outliers, can severely compromise the legitimacy of any quantitative analysis, thereby reducing the robustness of the resulting findings. When these biased data points enter the computational analysis, they can potentially lead to false conclusions and erroneous inferences due to the artificial relationships they induce between the variables under consideration. The reasons for such discrepancies range from errors in data collection to inherent inconsistencies within the sampled population. However, it is essential to bear in mind that outliers are not always problematic. These different data points can sometimes provide critical insights into the phenomenon being studied. Therefore, it is critical to understand the context thoroughly and to provide a compelling rationale for excluding outliers. More specifically, outliers could have an undue influence on expenditure and preference data. For example, an abnormally high value for expenditure could lead to an overestimation of the impact of public preferences. Similarly, outliers in the preference data could lead us to believe that there is a stronger or weaker correlation between public preferences and spending, when this is not the case. A well-known tool for quantifying the influence of observation within a regression analysis is Cook’s distance. This metric represents the cumulative change in the regression parameters when a particular observation is omitted, thus indicating the overall influence of that data point on the model. Observations that exceed three times the mean of Cook’s distance values are commonly referred to as potential outliers or influential data points (Barnett and Lewis 1994). Public preferences generally do not show exceptionally high values. Figure 3.8 shows Cook’s distances for the government spending variable.

Figure 3.9: Included countries per year

There are seven anomalous observations in my data, characterized by values that are more than three times the mean. These anomalous figures are all in the area of unemployment for a selected group of countries: Australia, Switzerland, Spain, France, Israel, Japan, and Norway, and all in 2016. The co-occurrence of these seven atypical data points within the same year and domain may indicate peculiar fluctuations in the unemployment rate for 2016, which deviate from the overall pattern of the rest of the data. I assume that these unexpected fluctuations do not represent phenomena that would significantly affect the correlation between public spending and public preferences. I have therefore decided to exclude these observations from my analysis in order to maintain the integrity and consistency of the data. Figure 3.9 shows the distribution of the remaining 370 observations in my data. When working with unbalanced data like this, it’s important to consider whether the unbalance could introduce bias or other problems into the model. The imbalance could potentially be problematic if it is not missing completely at random. As most of the missing data is based on data collection in specific countries, I do not see any systematic pattern that could be related to my relationship of interest. Nevertheless, as a robustness check, I will perform additional analyses using only complete cases.
