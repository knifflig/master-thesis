The empirical analysis provides insights into the substantive representation of different age demographics, focusing on the influence of public preferences on changes in government spending. The results, derived from a two-way fixed effects model controlling for country-issue and year, are summarised in the first set of models The presentation of the data takes into account the responsiveness to the median voter (H1) with and without control variables and the responsiveness to young voters in comparison (H2). Starting with hypothesis 1, which captures the general representation of public preferences, we find significant and positive influences on changes in government spending. A coefficient of 0.106*** indicates that a one-point increase in average public preferences is associated with a substantial and statistically significant 0.108 percent increase in government spending change. This result is statistically significant at a 99.9 percent confidence interval (denoted by ***). Consequently, I could conclude that overall policymaking is inseparably linked to societal demands, underlining the democratic values inherent in government processes.

My analysis aims to understand the relationship between public preferences and changes in government spending across policy areas, controlling for the influence of a critical control variable - government ideology. I define an increase in government ideology as a move towards a more egalitarian or redistributive government. When we look at the effect of government ideology on spending, we find a statistically insignificant positive effect. This means that there may be a tendency for more egalitarian governments to increase spending, but I can’t confirm this. I also control for the relationship between GDP growth and changes in government spending across sectors. GDP growth examines its impact on the relative shift in government spending between different spending sectors. In this context, the coefficients associated with GDP growth indicate the change in government expenditure resulting from a one percent point increase in GDP growth, holding other variables constant. While the effect is also insignificant, we see a negative trend. The assumption about the impact of GDP growth on government spending is that in a no-policy change scenario, an increase in GDP alone would lead to a decrease in the relative share of government spending in a given sector. This reasoning is based on the fact that my dependent variable is a relative measure - government expenditure as a share of GDP. Thus, an increase in the denominator (GDP) without a corresponding increase in the numerator (government expenditure) would inherently reduce this ratio. The comprehensive model shows negligible effects, consistently indicating that general expenditure in the sectors studied remains stable as a share of GDP. As a result, the reallocation of resources appears to be the main factor influencing increases and decreases in expenditure. These results suggest that the effect of GDP growth on relative government spending is sector dependent and has varying levels of statistical significance. However, it is beyond the scope of my study to investigate the underlying causal factors. First and foremost, these results underline the importance of adjusting the findings for GDP growth.

The empirical analysis shows that a one-point increase (between -100 and 100) in the median citizen’s preferences for increasing government spending leads to a 0.331*** percent increase in change in government spending, while a one-point increase in young adults’ preferences for government spending leads to a 0.222* percent decrease in government spending. The discrepancy between young adults’ preferences and policy outcomes can be understood in the context of dynamic and group representation. The key idea in group representation is that elected officials should physically resemble or share characteristics with the constituents they represent. In this context, the fact that an increase in young adults’ preferences for government spending leads to a decrease in government spending changes suggests that young adults’ interests are not adequately represented. First, it’s important to consider the effect of descriptive representation or the extent to which elected officials physically share characteristics with the constituents they represent. Research has shown that young politicians are consistently underrepresented in parliaments (Stockemer and Sundström 2018). There is also evidence that they behave differently from their older counterparts and are more likely to represent the interests of their contemporaries (Alesina, Cassidy, and Troiano 2018; Kissau, Lutz, and Rosset 2012; McClean 2021). However, the literature also suggests that simply sharing demographic characteristics with a group doesn’t always lead to an adequate representation of that group’s interests. In the early stages of their careers, young politicians may align themselves with the interests of their demographic group in order to establish credibility. As their careers progress, they may shift to other legislative efforts, leading to a potential disconnect between young adults’ preferences and actual policy outcomes (Bailer et al. 2021). In addition, young adults’ shift in values towards relational equality may not be well understood or supported by older politicians, leading to an increased demand for representation that is not reflected in the beliefs of the majority of parliamentarians. Therefore, there may be a misunderstanding or lack of empathy for the preferences of young adults, even though they may be known to older parliamentarians. Dynamic representation, on the other hand, focuses on how well the current political climate and policy outcomes reflect the changing values and preferences of the population over time. The decline in government spending despite an increase in young adults’ preferences for government spending suggests a lack of dynamic representation. The current political system, with its focus on immediate policy outcomes, may not adequately consider and incorporate the long-term perspectives and interests of young adults (Downs 1957; Strøm 1990; Arnold 1990; Jacobs 2016). This could contribute to a sense of political alienation among young adults, discouraging their participation in traditional political activities and creating a vicious circle in which politicians cater more to groups that are already politically active (Lawless and Fox 2015; Shames 2017; Pruitt 2017). The concept of dynamic representation suggests that the current low level of responsiveness to young adults is not necessarily a permanent state, but could change as the political system evolves to be more responsive to their demands and changing values.

Both theories are consistent in explaining my finding of low substantive representation of young adults, but highlight different aspects of the gap between young adult preferences and policy outcomes. Group representation draws attention to the potential inadequacies of descriptive and substantive representation, critical actors, and generational shifts in values. In contrast, dynamic representation emphasizes the importance of evolving the political system to better reflect the changing preferences and values of the population, particularly young adults. In order to better understand how to improve the representation of young adults, I will further explore these patterns in the context of different issue areas.

The results in the second set of models show a consistent pattern of government responsiveness, supporting a positive correlation between median voter preferences and changes in government spending. The model reports a significant base effect of 0.095, suggesting that each unit increase in median voter preferences predicts a 0.095 percent increase in government spending, independent of the impact of short-term issues. In the model for Hypothesis 3, I also introduced the effect of issue time horizons, using a dummy variable for short-term issue areas as a moderator. Despite the lack of statistical significance, which can be attributed to the limited sample size, the moderator effect shows an increase in the change in government expenditure of 0.054. Combined with the base effect, the total change in government expenditure on a short-term issue is therefore 0.149. This suggests a potential increase in responsiveness to median voter preferences on short-term issues. However, due to the non-significance of the study, the validation of this proposition requires further investigation. The potential increase in responsiveness to short-term issues supports hypothesis 3, which states that politicians are more responsive to issues with immediate consequences. However, given the insignificant statistical effect of the analysis, this result should be treated with caution. In the third model, I further included a dummy variable for issue areas that are especially important to young adults. The results reveal a significant and substantial increase in government spending relative to median voter preferences, with a base effect of 0.131 for other issue areas. The inclusion of a moderator variable for issue areas of particular relevance to young adults reduces the effect size of median voter preference by 0.075 percent to an 0.056 percent increase in government spending. However, the effect of this moderator, is not statistically significant, potentially undermining the statistical power to detect an effect. These results support my hypothesis 4, which states that responsiveness is reduced in issue areas of particular interest to younger citizens. My data suggest that despite the high spending preferences of young adults, their policy preferences are less likely to be reflected in government spending relative to the median voter. This diminished responsiveness to younger voters’ preferences is presumably due to the lower priority of these issues among the larger median voter population. In essence, this analysis shows that variation in policy issue areas does indeed influence policymakers’ responsiveness to different age groups. Policymakers’ responsiveness tends to be skewed towards the median voter, supporting my hypothesis and highlighting the role of demographic relevance in shaping policy decisions. However, it is also important to note the possible influence of short-term issues.

The introduction of the two dummy variables in a single model leads to changes in the effects of each variable. The effect of preferences becomes 0.118, while the moderating effects of the time and relevance dummies become 0.045 and -0.069 respectively. Both effects remain relatively stable. For a comprehensive analysis, I tested hypothesis 5 in the final model. When I considered all possible combinations of issue relevance and time horizons, I found that responsiveness to the median voter was significantly high 0.152 for long-median issues. Conversely, the responsiveness was -0.023 for long-young issues, 0.084 for short-median issues, and 0.410 for short-young issues. However, the last three coefficients lacked statistical significance, probably due to the limited number of data points. Hypothesis 5 posits a higher responsiveness to short-term issues relevant to younger citizens (short-young = 0.410) than to long-term issues relevant to median citizens (long-median = 0.152***). The results partially support this hypothesis. While we observe increased responsiveness to the median voter on short-term issues relevant to young people, this lacks statistical significance. Despite these uncertainties, the overall pattern of these results is consistent with the predictions. I have attempted to disentangle the influence of group representation and the motivations of politicians seeking votes on responsiveness. The coefficients are shown in Figure 5.1 in the context of the theoretical framework towards the median voter. Both theories suggest low responsiveness in long-term areas relevant to young adults. The negative value of -0.023 clearly supports this. I also observe significant positive effects for median voter issues, which also supports my predictions. However, it was indirectly predicted that short-term issues would yield the highest values for median voters, as both theories posit a positive effect in this regard. Surprisingly, long-term issues outperform short-term issues, suggesting the greater importance of group representation. If the higher score for short-term issues proves to be incorrect, this would further support this observation. My findings suggest that the domain of political issues can significantly influence the degree of political responsiveness. Policymakers show marked responsiveness to issues relevant to the median voter, particularly those associated with long-term interests. This can be attributed to a greater congruence of interests between the median voter and the typical policymaker in terms of age-related relevance and preferences. In addition, policymakers show a general preference for short-term issues with immediate benefits, although my results suggest that this may be a small part. Thus, the interplay between group representation and dynamic representation theories offers important insights into the determinants of political responsiveness in different policy contexts. I proceed with further investigating those interplays in the context of representation towards young adults.

5.2 Responsiveness to young voter

The third set of models presents the results related to Hypothesis 6 to 8, alongside a benchmark - Model 2. Model 2 shows that a one-point increase in young voters’ preferences for increased spending leads to a 0.222** percent decrease in government spending over the following four years. In stark contrast, an equivalent increase in median voters’ preferences leads to a 0.331*** percent increase in spending. This discrepancy illustrates the relative inattention of policymakers to the preferences of young voters. Including a moderator variable representing short-term effects results in no shift in the base effects. Thus, even when short-term issues are at play, every one-point increase in young voters’ preferences correlates with a 0.222 percent decrease in change of spending. This result, which is most likely not due to a lack of observations per issue area, suggests a negligible shift in politicians’ responsiveness to young voters when short-term issues are considered. However, this result contradicts my hypothesis 6, which predicted lower responsiveness in such scenarios. It suggests that the time horizon of policies does not significantly alter policymakers’ attentiveness to young voters, providing robust support for the suggestion that group representation dynamics play a more substantial role in shaping responsiveness and undermining arguments related to low political participation. To further substantiate this, I introduced a dummy variable for issue areas of particular importance to young adults. With the inclusion of this moderating variable for these policy areas, the base effect becomes -0.169* for young adults and 0.320*** for median voters. The impact of the issue relevance moderator on young adults’ policy preferences is -0.141, implying a 0.141 percent decrease in responsiveness when dealing with issues of significant relevance to young adults. This negative effect highlights the severe lack of responsiveness to young adults’ preferences, particularly on issues of concern to them, contradicting Hypothesis 7 and highlighting the need for increased descriptive representation. Further evidence of the importance of these drivers is observed when both dummy variables are included in the model, as the effect of group representation is significantly increased. To further explore the interaction between group relevance and time horizons, I included dummy variables for each combination (long-median, long-young, short-median, short-young). The coefficients are shown in Figure 5.1 in the context of the theoretical framework towards young voters. The responsiveness to young voters’ preferences is -0.150 for long-median issues, -0.334** for long-young issues, -0.219** for short-median issues, and -0.146 for short-young issues. Hypothesis 8 proposes that responsiveness to young voters in long-term issue areas relevant to median citizens will be higher than responsiveness in short-term issues particularly relevant to young citizens. This hypothesis is based on the framework’s assumption that in such situations, politicians have fewer incentives to adopt vote-seeking strategies and more opportunities to engage young citizens in conventional participation. However, the results show a different pattern: while responsiveness is negative in all scenarios, there is a particularly strong effect in long-term issue areas of concern to young people (-0.334***), further emphasizing the urgent need for adequate numerical representation in assemblies.

Moreover, the only other significant effect is associated with short-term issues relevant to the median voter. Policymakers vote-seeking nature is often connected with young adults’ lack of political participation, especially with low voter turnout among the youth. It has been argued that this gives legislators no incentives to cater to their interests. Young politicians on the other hand are suspected to be not able to adequately represent their contemporaries due to a lack of skill or systemic barriers within the parliaments and those who can, need to shift their strategies towards the older median voter to get reelected. This suggests that an actor who wants to get reelected has no choice but needs to ignore groups that refrain from voting. Thereby making a low substantial representation of young adults and other groups with low voter turnout to some extent their own fault. My findings show strong negative responsiveness for issue areas of particular importance to young adults, while short-term effects are negligible for politicians’ responsiveness to young voters, contradicting this hypothesis and providing evidence for the importance of group representation drivers. This shows that not vote-seeking strategies mainly drive policymakers’ considerations but shared characteristics. This highlights the need for mechanisms that address these numerical representation gaps and ensure that citizens are adequately represented in assemblies.
