Representation in turbulent times

2 A Theory of young adults representation

Figure 2.1: Three dimensions of representation (Pitkin 1967)

The categories of representation shown in figure 2.1, as introduced by Pitkin (1967), provide a structured approach to the understanding of the different mechanisms by which representatives fulfill their roles in a democratic setting. Descriptive representation is primarily concerned with the numerical equivalence between representatives and those they represent. The focus is on whether the demographic and socio-political characteristics of the representative body reflect those of the larger population. Pitkin’s notion of substantive representation, on the other hand, emphasizes the political influence of different social groups. It examines the extent to which the legislative process is influenced and shaped by different segments of society. In addition, Pitkin introduced symbolic representation, as the role model that legislators can embody, serving as a symbol of societal norms and values. This provides a useful framework for analyzing whether and how this demographic is capable of promoting its interests and sets the stage for my exploration of the substantive representation of young voters, a demographic group that is significantly influenced by political decisions, but whose political influence remains largely unexplored. Our study intends to critically interrogate the extent to which the demand for substantive representation among the youth is met within the policy-making process. By exploring this under-researched question, I aim to offer new insights into the substantive representation of social groups, and in doing so, contribute to a more nuanced understanding of the dynamics and potential avenues for mitigating democratic deficits.

The relationship between different types of representation is not straightforward and often depends on various socio-political factors. Mansbridge (1999) elaborates on the crucial link between descriptive and substantive representation, that descriptive representation assumes heightened importance in contexts where a social group has uncrystallized substantive interests. These interests are defined as those that are still emerging, under-articulated, or under-included in policy debates. Mansbridge argues persuasively that, in such scenarios, the presence of group members in legislative assemblies can promote more robust deliberation and recognition of these interests, thereby enhancing substantive representation. Recent scholarship has made a compelling case that the youth population is an example of a social group with uncrystallized substantive interests (Munger 2022). This thesis is based on the demonstrable difference between the political preferences of young people and those of older citizens, as well as the absence of young adults in political assemblies (Stockemer and Sundström 2018).

Furthermore, public opinion and government policy can be understood as reciprocal interconnected, creating a cycle of influence that is critical to the functioning of representative democracy (Wlezien and Soroka 2016). The links that connect this cycle are citizens’ preferences, their voting choices, the selection of policymakers, and the making of the policy itself (G. B. Powell 2004), which then feeds back to preferences. This chain has been shown to be exposed by numerous factors, most notably elections (Stimson, Mackuen, and Erikson 1995; Hobolt and Klemmemsen 2005; L. R. Jacobs and Shapiro 2000) and electoral systems (Hobolt and Klemmemsen 2005; Binzer Hobolt and Klemmensen 2007); issue salience (Burstein 2003; Elkjær and Iversen 2020) and interest groups (Baumgartner et al. 2009). Thus, this dynamic model of representation underscores a critical element in political processes - the adaptive relationship between public opinion and policy. Central to this perspective is the premise that political actors respond strategically to changing public preferences in order to maintain or enhance their electoral viability and that voters seek to maximize their own utility when voting (Downs 1957). The key driving force is the need to secure electoral support, reflecting the vote-seeking nature of political conflict (Strøm 1990; Müller and Strøm 1999). Existing literature has shown that younger demographic groups remain politically disengaged, as evidenced by a decline in conventional participation, particularly in voting and in joining key institutions such as political parties (Milner 2010; Goerres 2009). This interpretation expects a lack of representation towards young adults as policymakers have no tangible (electoral) benefits from responding to their interests (Lupia and Philpot 2005; Dermody, Hanmer-Lloyd, and Scullion 2010; Pruitt 2017; Raby et al. 2017). For a better understanding of the mechanics, I need to discuss what I define as representation of substantive interests.

The substantive dimension of representation involves the actions, decisions, and behavior of the representative, as opposed to descriptive representation, which primarily emphasizes the extent to which representatives mirror or describe their constituents in terms of shared demographic. As I explore the complex dynamics of youth representation, responsiveness, and congruence are two concepts often posited as integral to substantive representation (Wlezien and Soroka 2016). Responsiveness refers to how closely a representative’s behavior matches the preferences of his or her constituents (Dahl 1971), which is typically measured by comparing either government policies or actors’ positions with public opinion (Budge 2001; Binzer Hobolt and Klemmensen 2007; Ezrow 2010; Wlezien 2016). Congruence, on the other hand, emphasizes a match between the policy preferences of voters and the voting behavior or policy stance of their representatives (G. Powell 2009). High congruence implies a strong match between public preferences and political action (Boas and Smith 2019; Helliesen 2022). However, it is important to note that neither is a simple indicator of substantive representation, as constituents may not always know what is best for them or may not be able to express their preferences effectively due to systemic biases (Wittman 1995; Caplan 2008). On the other hand, representatives may want to take into account the interests of their constituents but may not be fully informed about how to do so (Lupia and McCubbins 1998; Jones and Baumgartner 2005). By its very nature, responsiveness respects the dynamism of policy preferences, whereas congruence assumes a degree of stability in policy preferences that may not always exist (Baumgartner and Jones 1993). While this assumption is useful in many contexts that examine a single, specific point in time, it cannot adequately capture this dynamism. The group of young adults constantly changes its preferences as people age and because of the fluidity of young voters’ political preferences and their exposure to rapid societal change (Jennings and Niemi 1975). This makes responsiveness a more appropriate concept for explaining the substantive representation of each age group independently, which is a critical aspect of relational equality. As Dahl (1971) states “a key characteristic of democracy is the continued responsiveness of the government to the preferences of the people”. This happens when the democratic process leads the government to formulate and implement policies that the citizens want.

2.1 Drivers of Responsiveness

Two dominant theoretical paradigms have been proposed to explain policymakers’ tendency to be responsive: (1) the conceptual model of group interest representation, and (2) the electoral paradigm, which emphasizes the vote-seeking tendencies inherent in political conflict. The theory of group interest representation posits that the behavior of legislators is significantly shaped by their personal preferences and individual backgrounds (Miller and Stokes 1963; Burden 2007). It postulates that intrinsic motivations, modulated by personal background and preferences, are the primary determinants of political action. This inference is supported by claims that legislators are naturally inclined to align with and advocate for demographic groups that are similar to their own, due to shared personal preferences or an affinity created by group identification (Mansbridge 1999, 2003). Legislators’ identification with particular demographic or social groups could significantly moderate their political behavior. For example, legislators who identify with a racial or ethnic minority may be predisposed to propose policies that favor their particular group because of a common identity or shared experiences. Life experiences also play a key role in shaping a legislator’s perspectives and priorities. For example, a legislator who has experienced poverty or who has worked extensively with impoverished communities may be more inclined to prioritize and support anti-poverty policies. Individual ideologies and personal values can also guide the political actions of legislators and influence their stance on different issues. An environmentally conscious legislator may be more inclined to support and propose policies that promote environmental protection, regardless of the political consequences or prevailing public opinion. Similarly, deeply held ideological beliefs can guide policy preferences and voting behavior. For example, legislators with a libertarian ideological orientation might favor policies that limit government intervention, while those with a socialist orientation might favor policies that promote government involvement in economic affairs. Empirical literature examining the relationship between politicians’ characteristics, such as gender, race, income class, and sexual orientation, and their propensity to advocate for groups with similar characteristics suggests a robust relationship. Evidence suggests that legislators are more responsive to the interests and preferences of demographic groups that share their personal characteristics (Whitby 1997; Tate 2003; Burden 2007; Carnes 2012; Franck and Rainer 2012; Broockman 2013), and this correlation has been extended to policy outcomes (Chattopadhyay and Duflo 2004; Preuhs 2006; Funk and Philips 2018). It should be noted, however, that these relationships are not uncontested (Ferreira and Gyourko 2014; Butler and Broockman 2011; Elkjær and Klitgaard 2021).

Another perspective on political representation, referred to as the dynamic representation model (Stimson, Mackuen, and Erikson 1995) (see the thermostatic model by Wlezien (1995) for a similar approach), emphasizes the ongoing, responsive relationship between shifts in public opinion and changes in policy. This conceptualization, often contrasted with static models of representation, asserts that policy does not simply reflect public opinion at a single point in time, but rather its constant evolution. The core idea of dynamic representation hinges on the temporal factor, illustrating a continuous interaction between public preferences and policy adjustments. Dynamic representation models suggest that political parties, in their quest to maintain electoral relevance, could strategically advance the interests of their electoral base to a greater extent. In support of this argument, it has been observed that politicians from certain racial groups may predominantly promote the interests of their racial constituents, particularly if they perceive winning support from other racial groups as more challenging (Grose 2011). The critical drivers here are the impact of upcoming elections on policy decisions (Hobolt and Klemmemsen 2005; Wlezien and Soroka 2016), just as the policies enacted also shape public opinion (Soroka and Wlezien 2009). Thus, a cycle of influence between public opinion and public policy is critical to the functioning of representative democracy. The assumptions that are made in this view are twofold. First voters can place all election alternatives along a political spectrum and choose the alternative closest to their own point of view (Downs 1957; G. B. Powell 2004). Secondly, Downs (1957) median voter theorem states that candidates rationally choose a point that maximizes their share of the vote and that the limited number of electoral choices (Downs initially studied the American two-party system) leads to an equal election outcome if both take the policy positions of the median voter. In this way, citizens maximize the utility of their votes and ensure that their preferences are reflected in the political process. These theories have subsequently been extended to include more candidates (Palfrey 1984) and in multidimensional political spaces (Davis, Hinich, and Ordeshook 1970). Central to this perspective, however, remains that elected representatives and parties are constantly faced with decisions about how to position themselves in the midst of these conflicting viewpoints. In order to be re-elected, politicians must maximize their votes (Strøm 1990; Müller and Strøm 1999) and align these positions with the preferences of the largest possible number of voters, usually the median voter. As such, they change their political positions dynamically (Stimson, Mackuen, and Erikson 1995; Wlezien 1995; Binzer Hobolt and Klemmensen 2007).

Traditionally, these dimensions have been studied separately. However, both these theories yield predictions fully consistent with scholars’ findings; indeed, they typically yield the same predictions. The dynamic model of representation thus emphasizes a critical element in political processes - the adaptive and evolving relationship between public opinion and policy. Central to this perspective is the premise that political actors respond strategically to changing public preferences in order to maintain or enhance their electoral viability. Group interest representation, on the other hand, emphasizes legislators’ identification with particular demographic or social groups. Having established the conceptual foundations of the dynamic model of representation and intrinsic group representation, the next step is to place these theories in the context of youth representation. The two theories have often made similar predictions, and both are supported by empirical evidence. However, as I attempt to disentangle the different preferences of young adults and how legislatures respond to them, I need to ask: what are the primary drivers of policymakers’ responsive behavior? The lack of research on responsiveness to different age groups represents a research gap with the potential for impactful insights. Age is an easily identifiable demographic characteristic that policymakers can immediately recognize and respond to, highlighting its importance in their policy choices. Consequently, the divergence in policy preferences between younger and older demographic groups may reflect potential differences in policymakers’ responsiveness to these cohorts, thereby contributing to a deeper understanding of the theoretical models under scrutiny. Answering this question requires a more detailed investigation. The approach I take here has two key elements. First, I discuss the theoretical expectations of differential responsiveness to youth. Second, I suggest that the crucial determinants of policy responsiveness vary according to the specificity of the issues involved.

2.2 Group representation

Given that young representatives should care about similar issues and best understand the demands of young voters, it seems obvious at first glance that increasing descriptive representation will lead to better representation of their interests. Indeed, there is empirical research that suggests that young politicians exhibit behaviors that distinguish them from their older counterparts. Alesina, Cassidy, and Troiano (2018) show that in the absence of term limits, younger mayors are more likely to engage in political budget cycles, perhaps reflecting their heightened career concerns and longer political horizons. While Kissau, Lutz, and Rosset (2012) find that the policy preferences of different age groups are relatively equally represented in the lower house of the Swiss parliament, the median preferences of citizens aged 21-30 are further away from the median legislator than those aged 51-60. A study by C. T. McClean (2021) provides some direct empirical evidence suggesting that Japanese mayors under the age of 50 increase spending on child welfare, ostensibly due to personal experience and longer time horizons rather than superior responsiveness to younger voters. Mansbridge (2003) contention of uncrystallized substantive interests provides a useful framework to explain the group representation of young adults.

Accordingly, the criteria for under-representation are met when a group (1) lacks adequate descriptive representation, (2) has distinct preferences, and (3) finds these preferences not fully deliberated in the policy-making process. While under-representation in political assemblies is a numerical fact (Union 2016; Stockemer and Sundström 2018), the shifting preferences of young adults have a long tradition in political science. One possible framework for explaining age-related differences in attitudes has been developed by Ronald Inglehart, who posits a shift in societal values away from material needs and towards self-expression, quality of life, and equality as societies become more affluent and secure (Inglehart 1977, 1990). This is being driven by an increase in higher-status jobs and as more cohorts grow up in relative prosperity, replacing generations of economic poverty (Inglehart and Rabier 1986; Inglehart 2000). Younger Generations, for example, appear to be more pluralistic, multicultural, and egalitarian in their beliefs (Abramson and Inglehart 1995; Busemeyer, Goerres, and Weschle 2009). Older generations, on the other hand, tend to be more conservative. They support greater military spending and oppose policies aimed at reducing income inequality in the US (Wattenberg 2020). These intergenerational differences have also been shown to influence party preferences in European democracies (Walczak, van der Brug, and de Vries 2012), with each successive generation showing a greater propensity to support Green parties, whose ideologies reflect quality-of-life values more than other left-wing parties (Kitschelt 1988; Lichtin, van der Brug, and Rekker 2023). It is important to distinguish generational and period effects from age effects. To help understand these differences, some researchers have examined age-related factors. Several studies have found inconsistent results regarding the alleged shift towards conservative views with age when controlling for generations and periods (Tilley and Evans 2014; Peterson, Smith, and Hibbing 2020; Geys, Heggedal, and Sørensen 2022). However, since my primary research question is how legislators respond to these differences, I will not further disaggregate these factors.2 In line with much of the research, I expect two main factors to drive the different preferences of younger citizens. First, younger generations are more likely to have more egalitarian and quality-of-life beliefs, and this is independent of point in time, as this is a persistent shift that holds true for each subsequent generation. Second, I expect preferences to be biased by the basic motivation of self-interest (Bowles, Gintis, et al. 2000; Fong 2001). When voters have to choose between different policies or allocate resources to different areas, they will be more generous if they personally benefit from them.

Mansbridge’s third claim is that uncrystallized substantive interests are not fully deliberated in policy-making. This could indeed be not the case, despite low descriptive representation. Politicians are embedded in societal structures that involve inter-generational interactions which leads to more direct contact between groups. These interactions, combined with more personal connections between younger and older generations, such as family ties, can significantly influence legislative decisions. As a result, it could be argued that, in the case of age groups, not only are the preferences of young adults well known to older members of parliament, but they also have an interest in and are better placed to consider, those preferences not in their own interests, but in the interests of their children. However, this argument undervalues the cruciality of shared experiences and beliefs for group representation. Even if older MPs believe they act in the interest of the younger citizens, they will never fully understand what those interests are. Additionally, I argue that if shared characteristics are the main driver of substantive representation, the majority of older policymakers are not willing to defend the interests of young citizens. That is cause the generational shift from material to immaterial values is not only reflected in different preferences but also in a shift from distributive to relational equality, meaning older generations will stronger favor the view that young citizens do not need substantive representation as they will be better represented (mainly in terms of material values) when they get older. Achieving equal representation for young adults, therefore, depends on a conscious deliberation that emphasizes relational equality between age groups, which can best be done by young representatives.

An additional factor that can translate a group’s descriptive representation into substantive representation is the presence of critical actors, a concept developed to understand the impact of women in politics. As explained by Childs and Krook (2009), critical actors catalyze policy development and stimulate advocacy for group policies, regardless of the numerical strength of the group they represent. This understanding emphasizes the importance of shared group solidarity and proactive efforts to consolidate this solidarity. It highlights the unique characteristic of critical actors, their deep commitment to group interests, and their conceptualization of representation in collective rather than individual terms. In addition, their potential to act as critical actors are enhanced by their rich political experience and their occupation of influential institutional roles such as committee chairs (Grey 2006). Following the theories of politics of presence and critical mass (Celis 2008), much research has identified women, feminists, and left-wing parties as the main champions of women’s issues in legislative assemblies.

However, recent research also highlights the knowledge gaps regarding non-group actors such as men (Celis 2006) and conservative parties (Childs and Webb 2012; Wiliarty 2010) in advocating for women’s interests. These non-group actors typically do not rally around group demands and thus do not advance group agendas, as understood from the advocacy perspective of parliamentary representation. These theories emphasize lived experiences, awareness of issues, and alliances with organizations as factors that enhance their advocacy. However, the concept of representation could also be reformulated as representation by deliberation (Celis and Erzeel 2013). In this model, a group’s interests are fluid rather than predefined and subject to ongoing political discourse. Critical actors are therefore deliberators rather than critical actors, contributing to dialogue and increasing responsiveness by introducing divergent and contradictory viewpoints. Focusing on the case of youth representation, systemic structures that favor existing age demographics (Joshi 2013; Stockemer and Sundström 2018) and perceptions that younger voters are not suitable for office (Mansbridge 1999) may hinder the rise of young representatives to the status of critical actors, limiting their ability to exert significant influence on policy-making and these fostering political disengagement. The critical difference to dynamic representation here is that the initial cause of this loop is the lack of representatives who reflect the interests of young voters in elected assemblies by virtue of their common age. Hájek (2019) argues that the age of legislators positively influences their parliamentary activity and voting, while longer tenure shows the opposite effect. Older and more experienced legislators propose more bills, make more speeches, and have higher rates of acquiring presidential posts. Therefore, even if the descriptive representation of young adults is achieved, their ability to significantly influence policy may be inhibited. But even if the opportunities to achieve influential positions are limited, older representatives cannot fulfill this need because they are not expected to understand the preferences properly. Only young representatives can act as critical actors, not by taking critical positions, but by deliberating on the interests of their contemporaries. In line with the large literature on politicians’ gender, race, (income) class, and sexual orientation (Schwindt-Bayer 2006; Burden 2007; Franck and Rainer 2012; Reynolds 2013; Broockman 2013) I expect that shared experiences and identities between young voters and their representatives will strengthen the link between descriptive and substantive representation, and consequently increase the responsiveness of policy outcomes.

2.3 Dynamic representation

Bailer et al. (2021) find that merely sharing descriptive characteristics with a particular group does not necessarily translate into representing that group’s interests. MPs, particularly those representing younger demographic groups, tend to strategically prioritize their legislative activities, initially aligning themselves with their group’s interests in the early stages of their careers, ostensibly to establish credibility and expertise. Later in their careers, there is an apparent shift to other legislative efforts. The implications of these findings extend into the realm of political alienation. When legislators do not act in the interest of their constituents this enforces low levels of descriptive representation - either as a direct result or mediated through institutional constraints - and catalyzes the feedback loop of political alienation (C. McClean and Ono 2020b). Younger demographic groups remain politically disengaged (Lawless and Fox 2015; Shames 2017) and show disinterest in conventional political participation, especially in voting and joining political parties (Milner 2010; Goerres 2009). However, the explanation of this limited political engagement as apathy (Dermody, Hanmer-Lloyd, and Scullion 2010) has been contested. A lack of political engagement may not simply signify apathy, but rather a sense of alienation created by a political system that is biased against the young and discourages their participation (Lupia and Philpot 2005).

Drawing on the work of Inglehart (1977), it is suggested that there is a noticeable shift in young adults’ values from material needs to ideals such as self-expression, quality of life, and equality as societies achieve greater affluence and security. This shift in values not only represents a difference in content but also reveals a different temporal perspective, which is a critical factor in understanding dynamic representation. In addition, the interests of young adults tend to be more oriented towards long-term decisions, due to the greater span of their potential future lives. This is underlined by the fact that younger Japanese mayors take a longer time horizon into account when making decisions. This inevitably means that they will be more deeply affected by the outcomes of such decisions (C. T. McClean 2021). However, the dynamic nature of electoral politics (Downs 1957; Stimson, Mackuen, and Erikson 1995; Hobolt and Klemmemsen 2005; Wlezien and Soroka 2016) and the constant need to appeal to voters (Strøm 1990; Müller and Strøm 1999) may lead politicians to focus on issues with immediate or short-term policy outcomes (Arnold 1990; Alan M. Jacobs 2016). These immediate policy outcomes can be effectively communicated and made visible in a short period of time, making it easier for politicians to showcase their achievements before the next election cycle begins. The recurrent lack of responsiveness of the political process to policies with potential long-term benefits, therefore, culminates in a sense of alienation among young voters. The cause of the alienation of young voters from the political process is understood here as a function of the incongruence between the long-term orientation of young people and the short-term orientation of political entities. However, this disengagement depends on young voters’ perceptions that their political preferences and interests are not adequately represented. Therefore, while descriptive representation may have symbolic value and enhance the legitimacy of democratic institutions in the eyes of young voters (Norris and Lovenduski 1994), I argue, in line with Pruitt (2017) formulation, that “while young people continue to create informal, individualized strategies to deal with issues they care about, many still want formal political leaders to listen to and address their concerns”. As politicians are constantly having to secure future electoral outcomes, they are unable to meet this demand if young adults refrain from voting. In terms of party recruitment strategies for political office, low youth voter turnout also affects parties as they have less incentive to nominate younger candidates (Norris and Lovenduski 1993; Parijs 1998; Berz and Jankowski 2022). Delli Carpini (2000) argued it like this:

[M]ost of the formal institutions of public life either ignore young adults and the issues that matter to them or are ill equipped to attract young adults and provide them with meaningful opportunities to participate. Parties and candidates see little reason to devote their resources to reaching out to young Americans given that this age cohort is less likely to vote than older Americans. Government officials are unlikely to listen to young Americans, knowing there is little risk that they will be punished for their neglect at the polls. (Delli Carpini 2000)

This second crucial mechanism of dynamic representation thus could become a critical catalyst of alienation (C. McClean and Ono 2020b). Analyzing this under-representation also requires consideration of voting behavior. Indeed, a meta-analysis by Eshima and Smith (2022) suggests that voters show a strong preference for younger political candidates, regardless of the voter’s age, which C. T. McClean and Ono (2022) confirms in an experimental setting. This finding challenges the conventional wisdom that voters are not inclined to elect young leaders and turns the focus back to the political participation of young adults. This argument is valid as long as policymakers do not derive tangible benefits from engaging young adults. This is underlined by findings that show young adults are highly engagement in new political forms (Pruitt 2017; Raby et al. 2017), like civil movements (Binder et al. 2020) and in issue-driven elections (Sloam 2018), when they perceive an issue as relevant. These findings suggest that the lack of responsiveness to young adults is not as deeply rooted as group representation theory would suggest, but rather a by-product of a political system that has yet to adapt to emerging values and expectations. Young people can provide both electoral support and resources to policymakers regardless of adequate descriptive representation, as long as they respond to their preferred forms of political expression, particularly interest group mobilization. While the emphasis on long-term preferences remains a systemic shortcoming, the dynamics of representation should theoretically allow for the adaptation and harnessing of young adults’ demand for representation. For example, according to the thermostatic model of representation (Wlezien 1995), the incentive to respond to these demands should increase in proportion to the degree of deviation from their preferences. Consequently, with increased responsiveness, the downward trend in political participation could be reversed (Sjoberg, Mellon, and Peixoto 2015), ultimately providing policymakers with electoral incentives to demonstrate responsiveness to the demands of young voters. Nevertheless, political participation remains a crucial determinant in shaping the supply side of conventional political processes (Norris and Lovenduski 1993; Lawless and Fox 2015; Shames 2017), as it provides little incentive to respond to their interests. While this could potentially be mitigated by adapting positions relevant to young adults, I don’t expect this to make a big difference. In addition with the consequent under-representation of young adults in OECD countries (Stockemer and Sundström 2018; Stockemer and Sundstrom 2022), I expect

Hypothesis 2.2 Policymakers to be less responsive to young adults than to the median voter, i.e. young adults’ preferences will have a weaker impact on policy outcomes.

While the potential drivers discussed agree on the importance of representing young adults, they differ fundamentally on the factors that contribute to the levels of responsiveness to young adults. While dynamic representation sees the problem as a more fluid process in which young adults’ patterns of engagement and the adaptability of the political system play an important role, group representation frames it as a structural one rooted in generational differences and the inadequacy of shared experiences in the existing political structure. Examining responsiveness across different issue areas can provide a more nuanced understanding of the drivers of political representation and responsiveness to different age groups, and thereby help clarify which theory offers a more comprehensive account of the key drivers.

2.4 Responsiveness & issue areas

It is widely acknowledged in the literature that different issue areas might involve different levels of knowledge, interest, and stake for different age groups. Young adults, for instance, might show higher interest and involvement in issues related to education, digital policy, or climate change (Abramson and Inglehart 1995; Kitschelt 1988; Lichtin, van der Brug, and Rekker 2023), while older generations might be more concerned with healthcare, pensions, or taxes (Sihvo and Uusitalo 1995; Goerres 2009; Naumann 2017). This variation can influence the responsiveness of policymakers towards the median voter (\(R_m\)) and young voters (\(R_y\)), as they might be more inclined to respond to demands or preferences that are more salient among their constituents. By looking at different issue areas, it is possible to evaluate whether the lower responsiveness to young adults observed in some contexts is a systemic feature (as suggested by group representation theory) or a consequence of political dynamics (as suggested by the model of dynamic representation). Understanding the nature of the issues allows us to consider the circumstances in which the representation of particular group interests outweighs the electoral interests.

To facilitate this examination, I have categorized policy issues according to their relevance to young adults. Categories are: (\(r_y\)) relevant for younger voters, (\(r_m\)) relevant for median voters. This segmentation recognizes that policy issues often resonate differently across age groups due to the different needs, preferences, and lived experiences associated with different stages of life (Inglehart 1977; Munger 2022) see discussion above] The first category (\(r_y\)), youth relevance, covers policy issues that predominantly affect younger demographic groups. For example, education policy, access to the labor market, affordable housing, student loan schemes, and certain aspects of digital or environmental policy fall into this category. Politicians are expected to be generally less responsive in these areas because they are not important to their groups (\(R_m(r_y) < R_m(r_m)\)). However as these issues are of particular value to the limited number of young representatives, I expect policy-making in these cases to be more responsive to young people (\(R_y(r_y) > R_y(r_m)\)). The second category (\(r_m\)), median voters relevance, refers to policy issues in which younger adults have no particular interest. Rather I expect these to be relevant for the median voter. As conventional political participation is low among the youth, this reflects disproportionately the older population. It includes policy areas that affect all age groups, with no particular emphasis on younger or older populations and those that reflect the specific needs and experiences of the elderly. These could be issues that affect the collective, such as general tax policy, national security, foreign policy or infrastructure development or pension systems, retirement policies, and health care for the elderly. In addition, some of these issues may be characterized by a high degree of complexity and interdependence with multiple national and international actors, effectively limiting the scope for policymakers to respond to a specific group. Given that the majority of parliamentary assemblies tend to represent older citizens, I expect these areas to be characterized by high overall responsiveness (\(R_m(r_m) > R_m(r_y)\)) and a particularly strong link to the preferences of older people, leading to lower responsiveness to young adults (\(R_y(r_m) < R_y(r_y)\)).

To adopt a comprehensive methodology, I introduce an additional temporal aspect, primarily dictated by the quest for electoral support. This aspect divides the political landscape into (\(i_s\)) short-term influential and (\(i_l\)) long-term influential issues. That elections, whilst providing a crucial safeguard of accountability, can also introduce short-term bias has a long history in political science and economics (Fisher 1930; Solow 1956; Nordhaus 1975; Arnold 1990). Alan M. Jacobs (2016) more recently argued that politicians’ myopia can be attributed to the desire for policies to deliver quick and visible results, which serve the dual purpose of demonstrating their effectiveness and increasing their electoral appeal. The pressing concerns and interests of the electorate are reflected in these immediate policy outcomes, creating a strong link between the electorate and their representatives. The information constraints and cognitive biases faced by voters play a crucial role in this process. Voters are more likely to be attracted to the immediate and tangible effects of policy outcomes, as these are easier for them to understand and appreciate. This, in turn, creates a strong sense of connection and affirmation towards the actions of representatives, ensuring that these actions are correctly identified as responsiveness by the electorate. Politicians, aware of this dynamic, are incentivized to design and implement policies that produce visible results within their term of office. Such a strategy is a means of securing public support and maintaining their political standing. The concept of hyperbolic discounting, defined as the tendency to prioritize immediate, smaller benefits over larger, future ones, is also relevant in this context. This cognitive bias pervades the individual decision-making process, but it also extends to collective policy preferences, leading voters to support policies that offer immediate gratification. Public mistrust in the ability of governments to deliver on long-term commitments can further reinforce skepticism about such policies. Nevertheless, a single-minded focus on immediate benefits can inadvertently fuel organized opposition to policies that offer future benefits. Policies whose costs are immediate but whose benefits are deferred provide an opportunity for interest groups to mobilize opposition. This dynamic complicates the policy-making process and discourages investment in long-term initiatives (Alan M. Jacobs 2011; Alan M. Jacobs 2016).

However, there is a significant contradiction in the practical application of this concept. Scholars often categorize an entire issue domain as either short or long-term (Bonfiglioli and Gancia 2013; Bonoli and Trein 2016; Schulze 2021), a practice that deviates from the theory’s original focus on preferences for particular policy instruments. In the case of welfare state policy, for example, the choice of instrument tends to confront policymakers not with the nature of the policy to be reformed, but with the decision to balance immediate costs and the time frame for potential payoffs. This inconsistency may be one reason for the mixed results of research on temporal bias in policy-making. While studies of specific policy areas (such as welfare state reform) often find evidence of short-term bias, this tendency is less clear when research focuses on resource allocation (Dahlberg and Lindström 1998). However, the primary aim of this research is not to identify systemic factors that may exacerbate short-term biases but rather to shed light on the differences in spending patterns across different policy areas. The main interest is in how these policy areas are uniquely affected by temporal dilemmas. It is not a question of whether a policy is inherently long-term, but which factors primarily influence legislators’ decisions when allocating resources in these areas. We hypothesize that in policy areas characterized by long time horizons for policy effects to become apparent, decision-making will be heavily influenced by long-term considerations, even if politicians are incentivized to find short-term alternatives. When comparing different policy areas within a single country, it is reasonable to assume that all these areas would be similarly susceptible to short-term bias, given that they are influenced by the same set of legislators working under broadly similar systemic conditions. This argument postulates that policy areas that offer numerous opportunities for short-term policy implementation will be driven primarily by such considerations, despite occasional instances of long-term strategic planning. In essence, policymakers are expected to opt for short-term policies whenever possible, and the differentiation between short- and long-term domains is determined on the basis of these opportunities.

2.5 What to expect from policy?

Short-term influential policies (\(i_s\)) can range across various domains, such as economic stimulus packages, or infrastructure projects. These policies are largely determined by the opportunities that these domains present for immediate implementation. For instance, a stimulus package can be quickly enacted to boost economic growth and building infrastructure is largely visible in daily lives. The immediacy of the outcomes associated with these issues enables voters to relate the actions of their representatives directly to the subsequent consequences. This fosters a sense of agency among voters, which influences their voting decisions. As a result, politicians are likely to be more encompassed by public opinion on these issues (\(R_m(i_s) > R_m(i_l)\)). Additionally, I expect that responsiveness to young adults in these areas will be significantly lower than responsiveness to older citizens (\(R_y(i_s) < R_y(i_l)\)). This is because younger voters are more likely to support policies with long-term effects since they will reap the benefits of these policies in the future. Additionally, the mitigating function of dynamic representation is less likely to increase traditional political participation among young voters in this time horizon. As a consequence, the comparatively lower turnout of the younger population is expected to play a significant role. In contrast, Long-Term Influential issues (\(i_l\)) are political matters that require a longer period of time to manifest their effects. These issues require a proactive, forward-looking approach, and the full extent of their impact usually not be apparent within a politician’s current term of office. Examples of such issues include tackling climate change or reforming pension systems. Politicians may be inclined to sideline these issues in favor of their short-term counterparts, even though the benefits of long-term policies may be considerable. The potential benefits of long-term policies are not only uncertain but also delayed, often becoming apparent after the current government has left office or even benefiting future governments. This factor introduces a degree of risk for vote-seeking politicians, which may result in less attention being paid to these issues (\(R_m(i_l) < R_m(i_s)\)). However, as these issues are of minimal relevance for the upcoming elections, I expect policymakers to be more responsive to young adults compared to short-term policy (\(R_y(i_l) > R_y(i_s)\)). The time horizon dimension underlines the inherent political trade-offs in policy formulation, which are constrained by electoral incentives. Policymakers are forced to weigh the immediate political benefits of short-term policies against the potentially greater but less tangible benefits of long-term policies. This framework allows us to systematically explore the responsiveness of policymakers across different issue areas and age groups. By mapping issue areas onto this matrix, I can conduct detailed analyses of how responsiveness varies across these categories. This, in turn, can shed light on the relative influence of dynamic and group representation theories in different policy contexts.

Scholarship on the substantive representation of ethnic minorities, women, (income) class, and sexual orientation has expanded over the years, with many studies focusing on a single country and issue (McBride and Mazur 2010; Kroeber 2018; Erzeel and Rashkova 2022). Comparative work across countries and issues for ethnic minorities, women, and sexual orientation is more scarce (some major exceptions are, Heath, Schwindt-Bayer, and Taylor-Robinson 2005; Schwindt-Bayer 2006; Bernauer, Giger, and Rosset 2013; Hänni 2016). A notable outline is the study of class differences, as several studies examine the variation in responsiveness to income groups across countries, issues, and years (Peters and Ensink 2014; Bartels 2017; Schakel, Burgoon, and Hakhverdian 2020; Elkjær 2020). Building on this methodological experience, I incorporate cross-sectional time series techniques. These techniques allow us to identify broader trends that cut across different national circumstances and temporal dynamics. Using two-way linear fixed effects regression (2FE) on data from 25 affluent democracies over the past twenty years, I aim to offer an understanding of the mechanisms by which representation in legislative assemblies and interest group mobilization of this population affect this central relationship.
