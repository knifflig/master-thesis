Representation in turbulent times

4 Methods

Statistical analysis often uses differencing to control for variables that remain constant over time (Hsiao 2022). This technique is particularly useful for understanding changes over time, such as changes in expenditure. By focusing on these elements that vary over time, the methodology effectively reduces the influence of attributes that remain constant throughout the analysis period, such as specific characteristics of countries or issues. This effect occurs because the unchanging effects of these attributes are eliminated by differentiating. A notable advantage of this approach is the reduction of omitted variable bias. This type of bias occurs when there are unobserved, time-invariant factors that are correlated with the independent variables under study. Differentiation over time can effectively address these unobserved influences. However, it is important to consider that these time-invariant elements may influence the nature or speed of changes in my dependent variable, despite their lack of impact on its absolute level.

4.1 Data structure

I assume that time-invariant effects related to countries or issues are of lesser importance. This assumption allows combining different levels within the data, which allows for studying time-invariant variation at the country-issue level while fully capturing time-varying effects. To identify the optimal model for the data and the research question, I construct a grouped index panel data structure with country-issue as an individual index and year as a time index. This construction facilitates the implementation of several statistical tests suitable for panel data of two levels (Hsiao 2022). Combining country and issue into a single variable streamlines the model, but this simplicity may obscure important information about the interaction effects between those levels. This can be a challenge when the influence of an independent variable on a dependent variable differs between different country-issue pairs. When looking at changes over time, I assume that most potential co-predictors are differenced out, but if there are factors that differentially affect changes in expenditure, a model is needed that can deal with them. Similarly, a time-series data structure allows the modeling of temporal dependencies, a feature that is crucial for understanding changes over time, such as the trend in government spending, but it is organized along a single time dimension. As such, it may not account for cross-sectional variation between countries and spending areas, potentially undermining the richness of the data available. Another strategy is to use multilevel or hierarchical models. These models can facilitate the inclusion of multiple levels of grouping variables and could thus accommodate both as separate levels. However, these models are not suitable due to their computational intensity and the resulting difficulties of data size incompatibility (Hsiao 2022). In addition, the potential problem of singularity may arise due to the overlap of preferences between different age groups (Gordon 1968; Elkjær and Klitgaard 2021). An alternative strategy is to use random-effect models with interaction terms between the independent variables and the country-issue index variable. These terms could capture the heterogeneity in the impact of independent variables across country issue groups. This approach not only significantly increases the complexity of the model but also allows for a detailed exploration of the variation in the impact of the independent variables across groups. However, due to the limitation of having only six observations for each country-issue combination, this approach may not be feasible.

If time-invariant interaction effects are expected, a fixed-effects model may be the ideal choice. This model eliminates the influence of these invariant characteristics, allowing the estimation of variables that vary over time. Fixed effects models do not assume consistent effects across groups and allow for individual-specific effects, allowing each group to have its intercept term (Hsiao 2022). This unique feature of fixed-effects models allows for the control of unobserved, time-invariant characteristics unique to each group. However, these models are not without limitations, as they cannot estimate the impact of time-invariant variables, as these variables are essentially differentiated in the model. Since I am mainly interested in how public preferences affect changes in government spending over time, this limitation is not influential because both variables change over time. However, this limitation requires careful extrapolation of the results, especially when generalizing beyond the OECD context, as the fixed effects of the country-issue combinations would potentially change. To underline my model choice, I additionally conduct Hausman-Wu tests (Hausman 1978) on all models.

In the context of a comprehensive analysis, it is essential to incorporate unobserved time-varying country or issue effects. This involves taking into account aspects such as time-varying country or issue effects that remain unobserved or have been excluded from the model. It is plausible to consider a variety of factors that are susceptible to temporal shifts, such as national policy changes or unprecedented events, such as natural disasters or political events of significant magnitude. These factors can lead to a significant change in a country’s performance in a particular area. A change in expenditure also falls into this category of concern. If spending patterns are sensitive to unobserved time-varying characteristics, these may go unnoticed or unchecked in the model. Significant changes in national policies, societal attitudes, or economic conditions that affect spending in the issue areas could also be missed. The inclusion of year-fixed effects can help control for unobserved variables that change over time but are constant across entities (like global economic trends), but these would not capture time-varying country or issue effects. However, the potential impact of these time-varying effects could be minimized if I used averages of a given period to smooth and reduce their impact, and their expression remained relatively consistent over this period.

The outcome in a given year might also depend on the outcomes of previous years. Such autoregressive dynamics may not be fully captured by the model. Despite the use of a four-year averaged change in expenditure as the dependent variable to adjust for some dynamic effects, complex dynamics spanning multiple periods may not be adequately captured. Averaging, while beneficial in smoothing short-term fluctuations and capturing long-term dynamics, may still require caution when considering autoregressions (Hsiao 2022). To regard for this with conducting breusch-godfrey tests (Trevor S. Breusch 1978; Godfrey 1978). If changes in my dependent variable do not maintain a constant variance across different panels (countries or issues) or over time, the situation may lead to heteroskedasticity. Heteroscedasticity refers to a condition where the variability of the error term, often referred to as noise, is not consistent across levels of the independent variables. This inconsistency violates the assumption of homoscedasticity, which implies equal variance of errors and is a critical criterion of ordinary least squares regression. Heteroscedasticity can inflate the standard error of the coefficients, leading to potential problems with the reliability of hypothesis tests (Hsiao 2022). I conduct a breusch-pagan test (T. S. Breusch and Pagan 1980) to control for potential heteroskedasticity and use adjusted standard errors that are robust to heteroskedasticity. Finally, the model may encounter difficulties with cross-sectional dependence, a phenomenon whereby changes or shocks in one country have an impact on others. For example, a policy change in one country could have repercussions in other countries. Neither of the models discussed takes into account the potential interdependence or transmission effects between countries in terms of changes in spending over time. To account for those I perform Lagrange Multiplier tests for spatial autocorrelation on all models.

Table 4.1 shows the empirical results of random and fixed-effects models tested along three dimensions: the Breusch-Pagan test for heteroskedasticity, the Hausman-Wu test for model specification, and the Breusch-Godfrey test for autocorrelation. In terms of the random effects model, as shown in the first row of results, the insignificant p-value from the Breusch-Pagan test suggested homoscedasticity. At the same time, the Hausman-Wu test indicates that the individual effects were correlated with the other regressors in the model. In addition, the Breusch-Godfrey test result indicated evidence of serial correlation in the residuals. Consequently, a fixed effects model would be more appropriate than a random effects model. In the second row, a pooled model has been tested and shows better metrics for autocorrelation. This indicates that the time dimension is of less importance than expected. The remaining fixed effects models underline that trend. The models show results similar to the random effects model in terms of heteroskedasticity and the Hausman-Wu test. While a time-fixed model can better account for autocorrelation, the twoway fixed effects model shows the best overall results. Nevertheless, we need to account for autocorrelation as the result of the Breusch-Godfrey test is close to 0, indicating strong evidence.

A series of supplementary F-tests examined the suitability of the fixed effects models, defined in this context as cross-sectional fixed effects, period fixed effects, or a combination of both. In this analysis, F-tests were used to allow a comparative study of two different panel data models against a pooled ordinary least squares (OLS) model. Specifically, the models examined consisted of an individual fixed effects model and another fixed effects model, but this time focusing on time effects. The aim was to determine whether the inclusion of fixed effects could potentially improve model fit and provide a significant improvement over the simple pooled OLS model. In addition, a fixed effects model that included both individual and time effects (referred to as two-way effects) was compared to the separate time and individual effects models. The results documented in Table 4.2 suggest that a two-way fixed effects model may be the optimal choice, as the null hypothesis for time-varying fixed effects was rejected. Additionally, the null hypothesis for individual fixed effects is significant at the 10 percent level. This hypothesis assumes that individual effects are uniformly equal to zero. Consequently, I conclude that both individual and time effects have a statistically significant impact on the model. Further analysis of the two-way effects via F-tests, alongside the models accounting for either individual or time effects, also revealed a particularly small p-value. This suggests that the fixed effects models that include both factors provide a better fit to the data, although a small deviation from the 0.05 significance level was observed for the individual fixed effects. It should be noted, however, that due to the significance of the Breusch-Godfrey test for this model, it is necessary to use autocorrelation robust standard errors as a countermeasure for this potential discrepancy.

Hence, in my subsequent analysis, I will use a two-way fixed effects model with the following equation:

\[y_{it} = \alpha + \sum_{j=1}^{N} \beta_j x_{jit} + \mu_i + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{it}\]

4.3 Regression Diagnostic

Figure 4.1: Assumption measurements

In the course of the empirical investigation, I carried out a series of diagnostic evaluations on the residuals to validate the assumptions of residual normality, linearity, and homoscedasticity. In regression analysis, it is assumed that the independent and dependent variables are linearly correlated (Hsiao 2022). It is also assumed that the error terms have a constant variance across the range of independent variables. The results of the tests for homoscedasticity and linearity are shown in Figure 4.1. Residuals, calculated as the discrepancy between observed and predicted values, should follow a normal distribution. A deviation from a normal distribution in the residuals could indicate an inadequate model fit or require a different data transformation. Normality tests, are based on a null hypothesis that the data are normally distributed.

If the calculated p-value is less than the selected significance level (usually 0.05), the null hypothesis is rejected, leading to the conclusion that the data do not come from a normal distribution. However, it must be emphasized that the decision to pursue the model in the face of non-normality depends on the severity of the deviation from normality and the particular circumstances of the application. The quantile-quantile plot shows a deviation from the line, and the formal tests of normality yield statistically significant results, it could be inferred that there is a violation of the normality assumption. However, as the standard errors are adjusted for autocorrelation and heteroskedasticity, but these changes are not reflected in the normality tests, the deviation should not be consequential (Hsiao 2022).

Additionally, an examination of the relationships between the independent and dependent variables has been performed. We intended to shed light on the dependencies and causal relationships present in the data, with a particular focus on the dependent variable of government spending and how it interacts with the independent variables of public preferences. I evaluate the hypothesis that the error terms are uncorrelated and have a uniform variance in my model. This is a fundamental assumption that underpins the validity and reliability of my regression. In this particularly revealing analysis, it is crucial to note that public preferences and years, as well as country-issue combinations, produce p-values that indicate their statistical significance. These statistical indices confirm the strong influence of country-issue combinations and years on the dependent variable. Based on these analytical observations, it can be concluded that the variables of primary interest contribute significantly to the explanation of the dependent variable.
